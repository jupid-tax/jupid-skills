# Dependents Reference

A dependent is a qualifying child or qualifying relative the filer can claim on Form 1040. Each dependent unlocks credits ($2,000 CTC for qualifying children under 17 with valid SSN; $500 Credit for Other Dependents otherwise) and may affect filing status (HoH requires a qualifying person).

**Legal basis**: IRC §151–152 (dependents and personal exemptions; exemption deduction itself is $0 post-TCJA, but the dependent definition still controls credits and HoH status).

---

## Two paths to dependent status

A person is the filer's dependent if they are EITHER:
1. A **qualifying child**, OR
2. A **qualifying relative**

Both paths have multiple sub-tests; ALL sub-tests for the chosen path must pass.

---

## Path 1: Qualifying Child

A qualifying child is a child, sibling, or descendant who meets all five tests below. This is the path most filers use — typical for parents claiming their children.

### Test 1: Relationship

The person must be the filer's:
- Son, daughter, stepchild, foster child, or descendant of any of them (grandchild, great-grandchild)
- Brother, sister, half-brother, half-sister, stepbrother, stepsister, or descendant of any of them (niece, nephew)

Adopted children are treated the same as biological. Foster children must be placed by an authorized placement agency or court.

NOT qualifying children for this test: cousins (they may be qualifying relatives instead), aunts/uncles, unrelated friends.

### Test 2: Age

ONE of the following:
- Under age 19 at end of year
- Under age 24 at end of year AND a full-time student for at least 5 months of the year
- Permanently and totally disabled at any time during the year (any age)

"Full-time student" = enrolled for the number of hours the school considers full-time, for at least 5 months (not necessarily consecutive) of the year. Online schools count if accredited.

### Test 3: Residency

Lived with the filer for more than half the year. Temporary absences (school, vacation, medical care, military service, juvenile detention) count as time lived with the filer.

Special rules:
- Children of divorced or separated parents: usually the custodial parent claims, but Form 8332 lets the custodial parent release the claim to the non-custodial parent.
- Kidnapped children: still considered as living with the filer who would have qualified to claim them.
- Children born or died during the year: considered to have lived with the filer the whole time they were alive in the year.

### Test 4: Support

The child did NOT provide more than half of their own support during the year. Note this is opposite of the qualifying relative test (which asks about the filer's contribution).

Support = food, lodging, clothing, education, medical, recreation, transportation. Do not count scholarships received by a student child.

### Test 5: Joint return

The child cannot file a joint return for the year, UNLESS the joint return is filed only to claim a refund of withheld taxes (no tax liability if filed separately).

### Tiebreaker rules (more than one person could claim)

If more than one taxpayer could claim the same qualifying child, IRC §152(c)(4) tiebreakers:
1. Parent over non-parent
2. If two parents (e.g., divorced and shared custody), the parent the child lived with longest
3. If equal time, higher AGI
4. If no parent claiming and multiple non-parents, highest AGI
5. Special agreement via Form 8332 in divorce situations

---

## Path 2: Qualifying Relative

A qualifying relative is a person who isn't a qualifying child but still depends on the filer financially. Requires all four tests.

### Test 1: Not a qualifying child

The person isn't the qualifying child of the filer or anyone else.

### Test 2: Relationship OR member of household

EITHER the person is one of these relatives (no residency requirement):
- Child or descendant
- Sibling, half-sibling, step-sibling
- Parent or ancestor (mother, father, grandparent)
- Stepparent
- Niece, nephew
- Aunt, uncle
- In-laws (mother-in-law, father-in-law, son-in-law, daughter-in-law, brother-in-law, sister-in-law)

OR the person lived with the filer all year as a member of the filer's household. This covers domestic partners, friends, anyone unrelated who lives with the filer year-round.

Note: parents and grandparents do NOT need to live with the filer to be qualifying relatives. They just need to be financially supported per Test 4.

### Test 3: Gross income

The person's gross income for 2025 must be less than **$5,200** (verify 2026 figure).

Gross income excludes: tax-exempt income, Social Security (mostly), gifts, scholarships used for tuition.

For 2026, verify the gross income limit against the latest IRS Revenue Procedure.

### Test 4: Support

The filer provided more than half of the person's total support for the year.

Support = food, lodging (FMV of room), clothing, education, medical, recreation, transportation. Government benefits (TANF, Medicaid) the person receives count as support not provided by filer.

For "multiple support" situations (e.g., siblings supporting an elderly parent), one sibling can claim if they provided > 10% AND the group together provided > half. Use Form 2120.

## Common edge cases

### Adult children living at home (post-college, working)

- Age 25 with $30,000 income → fails Test 3 of qualifying child (over 24) AND Test 3 of qualifying relative (gross income > $5,200). NOT a dependent.
- Age 22, full-time student, $4,000 part-time job → can be qualifying child if other tests pass.
- Age 30, disabled, no income → can be qualifying child (disabled trumps age) OR qualifying relative.

### Elderly parents

- Mom living in own apartment, $14,000 SS only, filer pays $8,000 of $15,000 total support → NOT a dependent. SS is excluded from gross income, so mom passes Test 3. But filer didn't provide > half support ($8,000 of $15,000 = 53% — actually does pass. Check carefully.).
- Mom living in nursing home, filer pays $40,000/year, mom has $5,000 pension → mom's $5,000 pension is gross income < $5,200? No, $5,000 < $5,200. Passes Test 3 if 2025. Also passes Test 4 if filer's $40,000 > half of total support (likely yes). Qualifying relative — claim with $500 ODC.

### Divorced parents, alternating years

Without Form 8332: the custodial parent (the parent the child lived with for the greater portion of the year) claims. That parent can release the claim to the non-custodial parent for any year via Form 8332.

Pre-1985 divorce decrees and pre-2009 decrees with specific language can sometimes be used in lieu of Form 8332, but post-2008 the IRS requires Form 8332.

### Married dependents

A married person filing a joint return is generally NOT a dependent (Test 5 of qualifying child; no equivalent on qualifying relative). Exception: if the joint return is filed only to claim a refund (no liability), the person can still be claimed.

### Newborns and deaths

A baby born December 31 is a qualifying child for the entire year. A child who died any time during the year is treated as having lived with the filer all year (assuming they would have qualified during their life).

### Kids of unmarried parents who don't live together

Custodial parent claims unless Form 8332 signed.

If parents lived together AND child lived with both, tiebreaker is higher AGI parent.

## Dependents and Form 1040

### CTC vs ODC checkbox

For each dependent in the dependents grid, check ONE:

- **CTC** (Child Tax Credit) — for qualifying children under 17 at end of year with valid SSN. Up to $2,000 per child for 2025. Refundable up to $1,700.
- **ODC** (Credit for Other Dependents) — for all other dependents. $500 non-refundable.

A dependent is either a qualifying child for CTC OR a qualifying relative / older child for ODC, never both.

### Dependents and standard deduction

Being a dependent of someone else REDUCES your standard deduction. If "Someone can claim you as a dependent" is checked on your own 1040, your standard deduction = greater of $1,350 (2025) or earned income + $450, capped at the regular standard deduction.

### Dependents and EITC

Qualifying children for EITC have slightly different rules than qualifying children for CTC — specifically, no support test and no joint-return test for EITC. So a child can be a "qualifying child for EITC" but NOT a "qualifying child for CTC" (e.g., 18-year-old non-student who lived with filer > half year).

---

## Required information for each dependent

For each dependent on Form 1040:
- Legal first name + last name
- SSN (or ATIN/ITIN)
- Relationship (e.g., "Son", "Daughter", "Mother", "Niece")
- Number of months lived with filer (used to verify residency test)
- CTC checkbox (if qualifying child under 17 with SSN)
- ODC checkbox (if not CTC but is a dependent)

If filing an SSN-less dependent (e.g., newborn whose SSN hasn't arrived), file Form 4868 for an extension to give time for the SSN.
