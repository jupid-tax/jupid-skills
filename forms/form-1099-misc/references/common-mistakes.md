# Top 1099-MISC Mistakes (with citations and fixes)

The most common errors agents and payers make on Form 1099-MISC, with the underlying authority and the fix.

## 1. Skipping 1099-MISC for a corporate medical provider

**Mistake**: Payer pays $4,000 to a hospital (corporation) for an executive's annual physical and skips 1099-MISC because "we don't 1099 corporations."

**Why wrong**: The standard corporate exemption (Reg. §1.6041-3(c)) does **NOT** apply to Box 6 medical payments. Hospitals, doctor offices organized as corporations, lab corps — all reportable on Box 6 if total ≥ $600.

**Fix**: Issue 1099-MISC with Box 6 = $4,000.

**Citation**: Reg. §1.6041-3(c) (general corporate exemption); IRS Instructions for 1099-MISC, Box 6 ("These payments are reportable to corporations as well as individuals.").

## 2. Skipping 1099-MISC Box 10 for a corporate law firm

**Mistake**: Payer settles a lawsuit, sends the $50,000 settlement to the plaintiff's law firm (a professional corporation), and skips 1099-MISC because the firm is incorporated.

**Why wrong**: Reg. §1.6041-1(d)(2) explicitly carves out attorneys from the corporate exemption. Box 10 (gross proceeds paid to attorney) is reportable regardless of attorney's entity type.

**Fix**: Issue 1099-MISC with Box 10 = $50,000 (the gross — full settlement amount). Don't issue 1099-NEC for the attorney's fee component (corporate exemption applies to 1099-NEC).

**Citation**: Reg. §1.6041-1(d)(2); IRC §6045(f).

## 3. Putting attorney's fee on Box 10 instead of gross proceeds

**Mistake**: For a $60,000 settlement with $20,000 attorney fee + $40,000 to plaintiff, payer puts $20,000 in Box 10.

**Why wrong**: Box 10 is **gross proceeds** — the entire $60,000 that passed through the attorney's trust account. The attorney's $20,000 fee component goes on **1099-NEC Box 1** (only if attorney is non-corporate).

**Fix**: 
- 1099-MISC to attorney, Box 10 = $60,000
- 1099-NEC to attorney, Box 1 = $20,000 (only if non-corporate)
- 1099-MISC to plaintiff, Box 3 = $40,000 (subject to §104 if applicable)

**Citation**: IRS Instructions for 1099-MISC, Box 10 ("Gross proceeds paid to an attorney. Generally, you are not required to report the claimant's attorney's fees. Instead, your report is the gross proceeds.").

## 4. Forgetting Box 3 for plaintiff's settlement portion

**Mistake**: Payer issues Box 10 to attorney ($60,000) but forgets to issue Box 3 to the plaintiff for the plaintiff's $40,000 portion.

**Why wrong**: The plaintiff received $40,000 of taxable income (assuming non-§104). The IRS expects this to be reported on a 1099-MISC. Without it, the plaintiff might fail to report, and the IRS won't have a cross-check.

**Fix**: Issue 1099-MISC to plaintiff with Box 3 = $40,000 (or full $60,000 if a gross-up reporting position is used in employment-discrimination cases).

**Citation**: IRC §6041; IRS Instructions for 1099-MISC, Box 3.

## 5. Treating residential rent paid by tenant as 1099-MISC Box 1

**Mistake**: An individual tenant pays rent to a landlord for their personal apartment. They wonder if they need to issue 1099-MISC.

**Why wrong**: 1099-MISC Box 1 reporting only applies to rent paid **in the course of a trade or business**. Personal residential rent paid by a tenant to a landlord is NOT 1099-MISC reportable.

**Fix**: No 1099-MISC for personal rent. The landlord reports the rent on their own Schedule E using their own records.

If the tenant uses part of the apartment as a home office and deducts the business-portion rent on Schedule C: that business portion is reportable on 1099-MISC Box 1 if the landlord is non-corporate and the business portion ≥ $600. (Most home office filers use the simplified method, which doesn't require a 1099-MISC; only those using the regular method via Form 8829 need to consider this.)

**Citation**: Reg. §1.6041-1(a)(1)(i) (definition of "in the course of trade or business"); Reg. §1.6041-3(c).

## 6. Using $600 threshold when OBBBA may have changed it

**Mistake**: Payer uses the old $600 threshold for Box 1 rents in 2026 because they read about OBBBA's $2,000 change.

**Why wrong (or right — needs verification)**: OBBBA Section 112201 raised the **§6041A** threshold (which covers 1099-NEC nonemployee compensation) from $600 to $2,000 for 2026. Whether OBBBA also raised the **§6041** threshold (which covers 1099-MISC categories like rents and other income) requires verification. As of last verification, **assume $600 still applies** to 1099-MISC unless IRS has issued contrary guidance.

**Fix**: Verify the current threshold per box at https://www.irs.gov/forms-pubs/about-form-1099-misc and https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/i1099mec.pdf before applying.

**Citation**: IRC §6041 (general info-return), §6041A (services); OBBBA §112201; IRS instructions and notices for 1099-MISC.

## 7. Filing 1099-MISC with January 31 IRS deadline (confusing with 1099-NEC)

**Mistake**: Payer files Copy A of 1099-MISC by January 31, treating it the same as 1099-NEC.

**Why wrong**: 1099-MISC has a **later** IRS deadline than 1099-NEC. For 1099-MISC:
- Recipient Copy B: February 1
- IRS Copy A paper: February 28
- IRS Copy A electronic (IRIS / FIRE): March 31

For 1099-NEC, all three are January 31.

**Fix**: Don't conflate the two schedules. Use the correct calendar.

**Citation**: IRC §6071(c) (acceleration of due date for nonemployee compensation only); IRS Instructions for 1099-MISC, "When to file" section.

## 8. Putting Box 6 medical payment in Box 3 (other income)

**Mistake**: Payer pays a doctor $1,500 and reports it in Box 3 instead of Box 6.

**Why wrong**: Box 6 is the dedicated box for medical / health-care services. Box 3 is a catch-all for other income. The boxes have different downstream tax treatments and audit risk profiles. Recipients may miscategorize on their return based on Box.

**Fix**: Use Box 6 for medical / health-care payments. Issue a corrected 1099-MISC if already filed: file the original (with $0 in Box 3) and a separate new form (with the correct Box 6 amount).

**Citation**: IRS Instructions for 1099-MISC.

## 9. Issuing 1099-MISC to corporate landlord

**Mistake**: Payer issues 1099-MISC Box 1 to a property management LLC taxed as S-corp.

**Why wrong**: Standard corporate exemption applies to Box 1 rent. Corporations and S-corps are exempt for rent payments.

**Fix**: Verify entity type from W-9. If C-corp / S-corp: do not issue. If individual / partnership / SMLLC: issue if ≥ $600.

Special case: a property management company that collects rent on behalf of an individual landlord. The PMC files 1099-MISC to the landlord (reporting rent collected and remitted). The tenant doesn't issue 1099-MISC to the PMC because the PMC is typically corporate. (Per Reg. §1.6041-1(e), the principal — the landlord — is the one with the income, and the PMC is the conduit; reporting requirements apply to the principal-recipient pathway.)

**Citation**: Reg. §1.6041-3(c).

## 10. Issuing 1099-MISC Box 3 for §104-excluded settlement

**Mistake**: Payer issues 1099-MISC Box 3 = $30,000 to a plaintiff for a personal-injury settlement that's fully excluded under IRC §104(a)(2).

**Why nuanced**: Technically, the IRS expects gross-proceeds reporting (Box 10 to attorney) for the entire settlement, regardless of recipient tax treatment. But Box 3 to the plaintiff is debated:
- Conservative payor practice: issue Box 3 anyway, let the plaintiff exclude on their return
- Aggressive: don't issue if §104 clearly applies

In practice, most insurance companies issue Box 3 to plaintiffs in physical-injury cases anyway. The plaintiff then attaches an explanatory statement excluding under §104.

**Fix**: Issue Box 3 if there's any doubt about §104 applicability or if mixed claims. Plaintiff handles the §104 exclusion on their return.

**Citation**: IRC §104(a)(2); IRS Pub. 4345 (Settlements — Taxability).

## 11. Forgetting state filing for non-CF/SF states

**Mistake**: Payer files federal 1099-MISC via IRIS; doesn't realize Pennsylvania requires separate state filing.

**Why wrong**: Pennsylvania (and a handful of other states) are not in the IRS Combined Federal/State Filing program. Payers with PA nexus must file state copies separately through PA's myPATH portal.

**Fix**: Check IRS Pub. 1220 Section B for the CF/SF state list. For non-participating states, file separately.

**Citation**: IRS Pub. 1220, Section B; state DOR websites.

## 12. Treating Stripe-collected rent as 1099-MISC Box 1

**Mistake**: Payer pays rent through Stripe to landlord; tries to issue 1099-MISC Box 1.

**Why wrong**: Stripe is a third-party payment network. Rent paid through Stripe is reported on 1099-K by Stripe to the landlord. The payer should NOT issue 1099-MISC Box 1 for the same payments — would be double-reporting.

**Fix**: Exclude Stripe-mediated payments from 1099-MISC issuance. Stripe handles 1099-K issuance.

**Citation**: IRC §6050W (1099-K reporting); IRS guidance against double-reporting.
