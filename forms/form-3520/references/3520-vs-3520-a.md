# Form 3520 vs Form 3520-A — Coordination

The two forms are siblings; many filers conflate them. This reference
clarifies who files which, when, and how the two forms must reference
each other.

## Form 3520 — filed by the US person

The Form 3520 covered by this skill is filed **by the US person**:

- US grantor / transferor (Part I)
- US owner of a foreign trust (Part II)
- US beneficiary receiving distribution (Part III)
- US recipient of foreign gifts (Part IV)

Due date follows the US person's income tax return (April 15 for
individuals, extended automatically with Form 4868 for the 1040).

Mailed to: Internal Revenue Service Center, P.O. Box 409101, Ogden, UT
84409.

## Form 3520-A — filed BY the foreign trust

Form 3520-A is the **annual information return of a foreign trust with at
least one US owner**. The trust files it (or its US agent does) — not the
US owner. It reports:

- Trust income, expenses, distributions for the year
- Trust assets and liabilities
- All US owners and their respective ownership percentages
- All US beneficiaries who received distributions

Due date: **March 15** (15th day of 3rd month after end of trust's tax
year). For calendar-year trusts, March 15.

Extension: 6 months via **Form 7004**, filed by the trust by March 15 to
extend to September 15. The Form 7004 must be filed by the trust, not by
the US owner.

Mailed to: Internal Revenue Service Center, P.O. Box 409101, Ogden, UT
84409 (same address as Form 3520).

## Why this matters for the US owner

A US owner files Form 3520 Part II to report their ownership of the
foreign trust. **Part II requires** that Form 3520-A is also filed for
the same year. The trust must:

1. File its own 3520-A by March 15 (with Form 7004 extension if needed),
   AND
2. Provide the **Foreign Grantor Trust Owner Statement** (page 3 of
   3520-A) to the US owner so they can attach it to their Form 3520

If the trust doesn't file its 3520-A — which is common, because foreign
trustees have no incentive to file with the IRS — the US owner must file
a **substitute Form 3520-A** as an attachment to their Form 3520 Part II.

## Substitute Form 3520-A

A "substitute 3520-A" is:

- A fully completed Form 3520-A using the same data the trust would have
  reported (income, expenses, FMV, distributions)
- Signed by the **US owner** (not the trustee)
- Marked at the top with "**SUBSTITUTE**" or "Filed by US owner under
  Notice 97-34"
- Attached to the US owner's Form 3520 Part II

The substitute is filed at the same time as Form 3520 (April 15 for
individuals, extended with the 1040). This is later than the trust's
March 15 deadline, but the IRS accepts the substitute filed on the US
owner's timeline as compliance for the trust's filing obligation as well.

### Inputs needed for a substitute 3520-A

The US owner must obtain or estimate:

- Trust EIN (apply for one if the trust has none — Form SS-4)
- Beginning-of-year and end-of-year FMV of trust assets
- Trust income by category (ordinary, capital gains, foreign tax credit
  available, etc.)
- Trust expenses
- Distributions to all beneficiaries (not just the US owner)
- Identification of all beneficiaries who received distributions

If the user doesn't have this data because the trustee won't provide it,
the user has a real problem: filing the substitute requires the data, and
not filing exposes them to §6677 penalty.

The IRS expects a US owner to make a "reasonable effort" to obtain
records from the trustee. Document the effort (emails, certified letters
to the trustee). Reasonable cause may apply to a §6677 penalty if the
effort was genuine and the trustee uncooperative.

## When neither 3520 nor 3520-A is filed

The penalty stack can be brutal:

- **§6677(a)** — 35% of the gross reportable amount for failure to file
  Form 3520 (Part I or Part III), with continuing 5%/month penalties up
  to 100% of the reportable amount
- **§6677(b)** — 5% of the gross value of the portion of trust assets
  treated as owned by the US person, for failure to file Form 3520-A or
  ensure the trust filed
- These penalties stack — both can apply in the same year

Real example: a US person who transferred $500,000 to a foreign trust and
never filed Form 3520 Part I or 3520-A could face:

- $175,000 penalty under §6677(a) (35% of $500,000)
- $25,000 penalty under §6677(b) (5% of $500,000 trust value)
- = $200,000 total, vs. zero tax liability

The First-Time Abate program does not apply (per IRM 20.1.9). The only
defense is reasonable cause under §6677(d), which requires affirmative
proof of due diligence.

## Joint filings — when one form serves both purposes

The IRS does NOT allow Form 3520 and Form 3520-A to be combined into one
filing. They are separate forms, separately due, separately mailed.

What CAN be combined:
- A single Form 3520 with multiple Parts (e.g., Part I + Part II)
- A single Form 3520 with substitute 3520-A attached
- A joint Form 3520 by spouses filing a joint 1040 (per Form 3520
  instructions)

What CANNOT be combined:
- Form 3520 and Form 3520-A (always separate)
- Form 3520 reporting two different trusts (file one Form 3520 per trust)

## Practical workflow

For a US person who is a grantor/owner of a foreign trust (the most
complex case):

1. **March 1** — Send a request to the trustee for Form 3520-A data and
   a Foreign Grantor Trust Owner Statement
2. **March 15** — If trustee files 3520-A: receive the Owner Statement,
   keep for Form 3520 attachment. If trustee doesn't file or doesn't
   respond: extend the trustee's filing via Form 7004 (filer's choice
   whether to do this on the trustee's behalf — risky if not authorized)
   OR plan to file substitute 3520-A
3. **By April 15** (or extended deadline): file Form 3520 Part II with
   either:
   - Owner Statement attached (if trust filed 3520-A), or
   - Substitute 3520-A attached (if trust didn't file)
4. **Save proof of mailing** (USPS Certified Mail) and a complete copy of
   the package
