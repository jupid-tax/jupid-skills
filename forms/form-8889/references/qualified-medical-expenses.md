# Qualified Medical Expenses for HSA

The list of expenses that can be paid tax-free from an HSA. Authority: IRC §213(d) (definition of medical care), IRS Publication 502 (comprehensive list with examples), Notice 2004-50 (HSA-specific Q&A).

If an expense is on this list, it goes on Form 8889 Line 15 and the related distribution is tax-free. If not, it's a non-qualified distribution — Line 16 — taxable as ordinary income plus 20% additional tax (unless 65+, disabled, or beneficiary of a deceased account holder).

**Receipts must be saved**. Not attached to the return, but the IRS can request documentation.

---

## Qualified expenses (HSA pays tax-free)

### Medical professionals

- Doctor (MD, DO), nurse practitioner, physician assistant
- Specialist visits (cardiologist, dermatologist, etc.)
- Dentist, dental hygienist
- Optometrist, ophthalmologist
- Chiropractor
- Psychiatrist, psychologist, licensed therapist, clinical social worker
- Acupuncturist (licensed)
- Christian Science practitioner
- Podiatrist
- Osteopath

### Treatments and procedures

- Surgical fees (medically necessary)
- Hospital and emergency room fees
- Outpatient procedures
- Physical therapy
- Occupational therapy
- Speech therapy
- Substance abuse treatment (including inpatient)
- Smoking cessation programs and prescription smoking cessation drugs
- Weight-loss programs **only if for a specific disease diagnosed by a physician** (e.g., obesity, hypertension, heart disease) — general fitness/weight loss does NOT qualify
- Fertility treatment (IVF, IUI)
- Vasectomy / tubal ligation
- Pregnancy tests, prenatal care, childbirth, postpartum care
- Lactation supplies (breast pump, breast milk storage bags)
- LASIK, PRK, cataract surgery
- Hair transplants for hair loss caused by disease (not cosmetic)

### Prescription and OTC medications

- Prescription drugs (including insulin, even though insulin is OTC in some states)
- **Over-the-counter medications and menstrual products** (CARES Act 2020 expanded the list to include OTC without a prescription) — this includes pain relievers, allergy medication, cold medicine, antacids, tampons, pads, menstrual cups
- Birth control (prescription or OTC)
- Contact lens solution, eye drops

### Vision and hearing

- Eye exams, glasses, contact lenses, prescription sunglasses
- Hearing aids and batteries
- Hearing exams

### Dental

- Cleanings, fillings, extractions, root canals
- Orthodontia (braces, retainers)
- Dentures
- Dental implants
- Crowns and bridges

### Mental health

- Therapy and counseling sessions (psychiatrist, psychologist, LCSW, LMFT, LPC)
- Inpatient and outpatient mental health treatment
- Substance abuse counseling

### Long-term care

- Long-term care services (in-home care, nursing home if medically necessary)
- Long-term care insurance premiums — **subject to age-based annual limits** (Pub 502 Table 21):
  - Age 40 or under: $480 (2024) — verify each year
  - Age 41–50: ~$890
  - Age 51–60: ~$1,790
  - Age 61–70: ~$4,770
  - Age 71+: ~$5,960

### Health insurance premiums (limited cases)

Generally, health insurance premiums are NOT qualified medical expenses for HSA purposes. **Exceptions:**

- **COBRA continuation coverage** premiums (when unemployed or recently terminated)
- **Health insurance premiums while collecting unemployment** (state or federal)
- **Long-term care insurance premiums** (subject to age limits above)
- **Medicare Part A, B, D premiums** — only after age 65; **NOT Medigap**
- Medicare Advantage (Part C) premiums after 65
- Health insurance for the unemployed/uninsured collecting unemployment compensation
- Healthcare premiums while receiving federal/state unemployment

**Individual-market premiums (Healthcare.gov) for working-age filers** are NOT qualified. This catches many users who try to use HSA for ACA marketplace premiums while still employed.

### Medical equipment and supplies

- Crutches, wheelchairs, walkers
- Blood glucose monitors, test strips, lancets
- CPAP machine and supplies
- Hearing aids and batteries
- Oxygen
- Bandages, first aid supplies
- Thermometers, blood pressure monitors
- Pregnancy and ovulation tests

### Other

- Service animal expenses (for the disabled)
- Special education for a child with learning disabilities (recommended by a doctor)
- Drug addiction treatment
- Capital improvements to a home for medical reasons (e.g., wheelchair ramp, widened doorways) — only the cost above the increase in home value
- Travel for medical care: mileage at the medical rate (21 cents/mile in 2024 — verify yearly), tolls, parking, lodging up to $50/night per person if traveling for medical care
- Funeral expenses are NOT qualified, but burial plot is NOT either

---

## NOT qualified (taxable + 20% penalty if under 65)

- Cosmetic surgery (unless for a deformity, congenital abnormality, accident, or disfiguring disease)
- Botox for cosmetic purposes
- Teeth whitening (cosmetic)
- Hair transplants (cosmetic)
- Gym membership, fitness classes (unless prescribed for a specific disease)
- Vitamins and supplements (unless prescribed for a specific medical condition)
- Toiletries (toothpaste, soap, deodorant)
- Cosmetics, makeup
- Health club dues
- Funeral expenses
- Diaper service for healthy infants
- Maternity clothes
- Childcare for a healthy child
- Marijuana / cannabis (federally illegal; not qualified even if state-legal medical marijuana)
- Nutritional supplements not prescribed
- Insurance premiums (other than the limited exceptions listed above)
- Medigap / Medicare Supplement premiums (different from Medicare A/B/D)
- Most cosmetic dental work (whitening, veneers for appearance)

## Special rules

### Family member expenses

HSA can pay tax-free for medical expenses of:

- The HSA account holder
- The account holder's spouse (even if covered under a different plan)
- Tax dependents of the account holder

Important: the family member need NOT be covered under the user's HDHP. A user with self-only HDHP can still pay for their spouse's qualified medical expenses tax-free from the HSA (the spouse is just NOT a covered person under the HDHP).

### Timing of expenses vs. HSA establishment

An expense qualifies only if it was **incurred after the HSA was established**. The "established" date is when the HSA was funded — not when it was opened. State trust law (typically Massachusetts or wherever the custodian is chartered) governs.

A user who opened an HSA in March 2024 but didn't fund it until July 2024 has an HSA establishment date of July 2024. Medical expenses from January–June 2024 cannot be reimbursed tax-free from this HSA.

**Best practice**: fund the HSA with at least $1 the moment it's opened, even if the bulk of contributions come later.

### Open-ended reimbursement

The IRS imposes **no statute of limitations** on HSA reimbursements as long as the expense was incurred after the HSA establishment date and you can document it. A user can pay a $300 dentist bill from cash in 2026, save the receipt, and reimburse themselves from the HSA in 2056 — tax-free.

This is the foundation of the "investment HSA" strategy — pay current medical bills from cash, save receipts, let the HSA balance compound for decades, withdraw tax-free in retirement.

### After age 65

After the account holder turns 65:

- Qualified medical expenses are still tax-free (no change)
- Non-qualified distributions are taxable as ordinary income but **no 20% penalty**
- The HSA functions like a Traditional IRA for non-medical uses after 65

This is why the HSA is sometimes called the "stealth retirement account" — it provides Traditional IRA-style behavior at 65 plus tax-free withdrawals for medical expenses (which are virtually unlimited in retirement).

### Documentation requirements

For each qualified medical expense paid from the HSA, keep:

- The receipt or invoice from the provider
- Date of service (must be after HSA establishment)
- Provider name, address
- Description of the service (e.g., "office visit", "dental cleaning", "prescription")
- Amount paid

For OTC medications without a clear receipt category, save the cash register receipt with the medication name circled.

For prescriptions, the pharmacy receipt suffices.

For mileage to medical appointments, keep a log of date, destination, miles driven, and purpose.

These records support the entries on Line 15 if the IRS audits.

## Decision tree for the agent

When the user describes an expense, walk:

1. Was the expense **incurred after the HSA establishment date**? If no → not qualified.
2. Is it on the qualified list above? If yes → qualified.
3. Is it on the NOT qualified list? If yes → not qualified, goes to Line 16.
4. Is it ambiguous (gym membership "for blood pressure", supplements "recommended by doctor")? → Ask for documentation. A general doctor's note is usually NOT enough; the user needs a Letter of Medical Necessity describing the specific condition and why the item treats it. Without that, treat as not qualified.
5. Is it a health insurance premium? → Apply the limited-exception list (COBRA, unemployment, long-term care, post-65 Medicare). All others → not qualified.

When in doubt, **ASK** the user for the documentation rather than guess. Wrongly classifying an expense as qualified can result in CP2000 penalties; wrongly classifying as non-qualified just costs the user the deduction.
