# Qualified Education Expenses for 529 and Coverdell

The complete list of what counts as a Qualified Higher Education Expense (QHEE) for 529 plans and qualified expenses for Coverdell ESAs. Reference Pub 970 and IRC §529(e)(3) / §530(b)(3).

If an expense doesn't appear on the qualified list, it's non-qualified. The dollar amount of the distribution matched against non-qualified expenses produces a taxable earnings allocation. See [`aaqee-adjustment.md`](./aaqee-adjustment.md).

---

## 529 plan — Qualified Higher Education Expenses (IRC §529(e)(3))

### Tuition and required fees

**Qualified:**
- Tuition paid to an eligible educational institution (any college, university, vocational school eligible to participate in federal student aid programs under HEA Title IV)
- Mandatory enrollment fees, registration fees, lab fees that are required for enrollment or attendance
- Eligibility verified at the [Department of Education's school code search](https://fafsa.ed.gov/spa/fafsa/help/ftthome.htm)

**Not qualified:**
- Optional fees (gym membership, optional health insurance, parking, recreation pass)
- Fees for non-credit recreational courses
- Late fees, parking tickets, fines

### Books, supplies, and equipment required for courses

**Qualified:**
- Required textbooks (verify against course syllabus or bookstore "required" list)
- Required supplies (lab manuals, art supplies for an art course, etc.)
- Required equipment (specific calculators, instruments for a music course, scrubs for nursing program)

**Not qualified:**
- "Recommended" but not required books
- General-purpose supplies the student also uses outside the course
- Equipment for non-academic purposes

### Computer technology and internet access (since 2015)

Per IRC §529(e)(3)(A)(iii), added by the Protecting Americans from Tax Hikes Act of 2015:

**Qualified:**
- Computer or peripheral equipment
- Computer software (used primarily by the beneficiary while enrolled)
- Internet access and related services

The "**primarily by the beneficiary**" test is the key constraint. A family computer used 50% by the student and 50% by siblings/parents is partially qualified — the beneficiary-use portion only.

**Not qualified:**
- Software for sports, games, or hobbies (unless predominantly educational)
- Computer used primarily by family members other than the beneficiary

### Room and board (only if half-time enrollment)

**Qualified, but capped:**
- Room and board incurred while the beneficiary is enrolled at least **half-time** in an eligible institution
- Capped at the **lesser of**:
  - The school's published cost of attendance allowance for room and board, or
  - The actual amount charged for on-campus housing
- For students living off-campus or with parents, the cap is the school's published "off-campus" or "with parents" cost-of-attendance figure

**Not qualified:**
- Any portion exceeding the cost of attendance cap
- Room and board if the beneficiary is enrolled less than half-time
- Travel between school and home

### Special needs services

**Qualified:**
- Special needs services (e.g., for a disabled beneficiary) required for the beneficiary to attend an eligible institution

### K-12 tuition (since TCJA 2018, capped)

Per IRC §529(c)(7), added by TCJA:

**Qualified:**
- Tuition paid to a public, private, or religious elementary or secondary school
- **Capped at $10,000 per beneficiary per year** across all 529 accounts for that beneficiary

**Not qualified for 529 K-12:**
- K-12 books, supplies, equipment, or room and board (only tuition counts at the K-12 level for 529)
- Tuition above the $10,000/year cap

For Coverdell ESA, K-12 expenses are MUCH broader (see Coverdell section below).

### Apprenticeship costs (since SECURE Act 2019)

Per IRC §529(c)(8), added by the SECURE Act of 2019:

**Qualified:**
- Fees, books, supplies, and equipment required for participation in a registered apprenticeship program
- Program must be registered and certified by the Secretary of Labor under section 1 of the National Apprenticeship Act

**Not qualified:**
- Non-registered programs
- Optional materials not required by the apprenticeship

### Qualified student loan repayment (since SECURE Act 2019, capped)

Per IRC §529(c)(9), added by the SECURE Act of 2019:

**Qualified:**
- Principal or interest payments on a qualified student loan (defined in IRC §221(d)(1)) of the beneficiary or a sibling
- **$10,000 lifetime cap per beneficiary** (and a separate $10,000 lifetime cap per sibling)

**Not qualified:**
- Student loans of the account owner (unless they are also the beneficiary)
- Student loans of cousins, parents, or other relatives
- Loans that don't meet the §221(d)(1) qualified student loan definition (e.g., personal loans used for education)

**Watch:** Interest paid with 529 funds is NOT also deductible under the student loan interest deduction (IRC §221(e)(1)). Coordination required.

### 529-to-Roth IRA rollover (since SECURE 2.0 §126, distributions after 2023)

Per IRC §529(c)(3)(E), added by SECURE 2.0:

This is treated as a *qualified distribution* — non-taxable, no 10% additional tax — only if **all** the conditions in [`secure-2-roth-rollover.md`](./secure-2-roth-rollover.md) are met.

## Coverdell ESA — Qualified expenses (IRC §530(b)(3))

Coverdell ESA expenses are **broader than 529 expenses**, particularly for K-12.

### K-12 (elementary and secondary, public/private/religious)

**Qualified for Coverdell K-12:**
- Tuition and fees
- Books, supplies, and equipment required for enrollment or attendance
- Tutoring services
- Special needs services for a special needs beneficiary
- Room and board if living away from home (e.g., boarding school)
- Uniforms required by the school
- Transportation between home and school
- Supplementary items and services (e.g., extended-day programs)
- Computer technology, equipment, internet access (for the beneficiary's use during enrollment)

**Cap:** No federal cap on Coverdell K-12 expenses (the $10,000/year cap applies only to 529 K-12, not Coverdell).

### Higher education

Same as 529 QHEE list above (tuition, fees, required books/supplies/equipment, room and board if half-time+, computer technology, special needs services).

**Differences from 529:**
- Coverdell does NOT include the §529(c)(8) apprenticeship expansion (some practitioners argue otherwise; verify with current Pub 970)
- Coverdell does NOT include the §529(c)(9) student loan repayment expansion
- Coverdell does NOT include the SECURE 2.0 529-to-Roth rollover (529 only)

## Eligible educational institution (both 529 and Coverdell higher ed)

An eligible institution is one that:

- Is eligible to participate in federal student aid programs under Title IV of the Higher Education Act
- Is on the U.S. Department of Education school code list ([fafsa.gov code search](https://fafsa.ed.gov/spa/fafsa/help/ftthome.htm))

This includes:
- Most U.S. accredited 4-year and 2-year colleges and universities (public and private)
- Most accredited vocational and proprietary schools
- Some foreign schools that participate in federal student aid (limited list)

**Not eligible:**
- Schools without Title IV eligibility (some test-prep, coaching, and unaccredited programs)
- Most foreign schools (the list of eligible foreign schools is short — verify before claiming)

## Items that look qualified but are NOT

These trip up filers regularly:

| Item | Why not qualified |
|------|------|
| Transportation (commuting, plane tickets home for breaks) | Not in IRC §529(e)(3) list |
| Health insurance | Not in IRC §529(e)(3) list (even if required by the school) |
| Optional sports equipment, gym membership | Not "required for enrollment" |
| Cell phone (unless required by the school for academic purposes) | Generally not "computer technology" used primarily for course |
| Furniture for off-campus apartment | Not room and board; not within cost-of-attendance for furnished housing |
| Test prep (LSAT, MCAT, GRE) | Not for enrolled student in eligible institution |
| Application fees | Not enrollment-conditional fee at an eligible institution where beneficiary actually enrolls |
| Senior trip, study abroad costs not part of tuition | Generally optional; not within cost-of-attendance for required housing |
| Music lessons unrelated to coursework | Not required course supply |

## Coordination with other education benefits

The same dollar of qualified expense **cannot** be used for both:

1. A tax-free 529 distribution AND a tax credit (AOTC or Lifetime Learning Credit)
2. A tax-free 529 distribution AND a tax-free scholarship
3. A tax-free 529 distribution AND employer education assistance (excluded from income under IRC §127)
4. A tax-free 529 distribution AND the deduction (currently the deduction is suspended; verify)

The user must allocate. See [`aaqee-adjustment.md`](./aaqee-adjustment.md) for the calculation.

The strategy that maximizes total benefit usually:
1. Apply scholarships and grants first (they're tax-free regardless)
2. Reserve up to $4,000 of qualified expenses for AOTC (the credit is worth up to $2,500 — usually a better return than tax-free 529)
3. Reserve next $10,000 for LLC if AOTC is unavailable (the credit is up to $2,000)
4. Use 529 distributions for the remainder

This is a planning consideration, not a filing one. The skill processes whatever the user actually did, not what they should have done.
