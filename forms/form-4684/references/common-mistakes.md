# Common Form 4684 Mistakes (Audit-Trip List)

The top mistakes the IRS catches on Form 4684, with citations and fixes. Use this checklist when reviewing a draft before submission.

---

## 1. Claiming a Section A loss without a federally declared disaster (post-TCJA)

**The error**: Filing Section A for a personal-use loss (house fire, theft, vandalism, vehicle accident) where the event was NOT a federally declared disaster.

**Why wrong**: IRC §165(h)(5), enacted in TCJA, restricts personal-use casualty deductions for tax years 2018-2025 to losses attributable to federally declared disasters. A house fire that wasn't part of a declared disaster is not deductible.

**Fix**: Confirm FEMA-DR / EM declaration before filing. If no declaration covers the event, the loss is not deductible — do not file Form 4684 Section A. Verify at https://www.fema.gov/disaster/declarations.

**Audit trigger**: The IRS routinely cross-checks FEMA disaster numbers. A claim without a valid FEMA reference or in a non-declared geography will draw a CP2000 notice.

---

## 2. Missing the FEMA disaster declaration number on the form

**The error**: Section A claimed but the FEMA-DR / EM number field at the top of the form is blank.

**Why wrong**: The IRS requires the specific declaration number to verify the loss qualifies under §165(h)(5).

**Fix**: Every Section A filing must include "DR-####-XX" or "EM-####-XX" on the header. Look up the correct number for the user's state and event date at FEMA.

**Audit trigger**: E-file may auto-reject the return; paper filings get a manual review and notice.

---

## 3. Applying the $100 floor per item instead of per event

**The error**: Hurricane damages roof, vehicle, and fence. User subtracts $100 from each property column on Form 4684.

**Why wrong**: IRC §165(h)(1) is "$100 per casualty," not per item. One event = one $100 reduction, regardless of how many properties were damaged in that event.

**Fix**: Sum all items in a single event first (Line 10 total), then subtract $100 once on Line 11. If multiple separate events, file additional Forms 4684 (one per event) — each gets its own $100.

**Citation**: IRC §165(h)(1); Pub 547 "$100 Rule"; Reg. §1.165-7(b)(4).

---

## 4. Applying the 10% AGI floor multiple times

**The error**: User has two separate casualties on the same return and applies a 10% AGI floor to each event.

**Why wrong**: IRC §165(h)(2)(A) applies the 10% AGI floor ONCE across all personal-use casualty losses on the return for the year, after summing all events.

**Fix**: Roll up all Section A losses (after each event's $100) on Line 16, then subtract 10% AGI ONCE on Line 17.

**Citation**: IRC §165(h)(2)(A); Pub 547 "10% Rule".

---

## 5. Using replacement cost or insured value instead of adjusted basis

**The error**: User enters the cost to rebuild ($350,000) on Line 2 instead of the original purchase price plus improvements ($180,000).

**Why wrong**: Reg. §1.165-7(b)(1) caps the deductible loss at adjusted basis (purchase price + improvements − prior depreciation). Replacement cost is irrelevant.

**Fix**: Use adjusted basis — receipts from purchase, closing statement, improvement records, depreciation schedules. Do not use Zillow estimates, insured value, or replacement-cost estimates as basis.

**Audit trigger**: A loss claim that exceeds reasonable basis (e.g., $350K loss on a property purchased for $180K) draws scrutiny.

---

## 6. Not subtracting expected insurance reimbursement when claim is pending

**The error**: User reports the full loss on Line 2/3 without reducing for insurance because the claim hasn't paid out yet.

**Why wrong**: Pub 547 requires reduction by reimbursement that's been received OR is reasonably foreseeable. If the user has filed a claim and a recovery is likely, the expected amount must be subtracted.

**Fix**: If the claim is pending and a recovery is likely, estimate and subtract. If the final amount differs from the estimate, file an amended return. If no estimate is reasonable, defer the entire loss until the year of resolution.

**Citation**: Pub 547 ("Insurance and Other Reimbursements"); Reg. §1.165-1(d)(2)(i).

---

## 7. Failing to file an insurance claim when insured

**The error**: User had homeowners insurance but didn't file a claim (e.g., to avoid a premium hike). Reports the full loss on Form 4684.

**Why wrong**: Pub 547 reduces the deductible loss by the amount insurance WOULD have reimbursed if the user had filed. Choosing not to file doesn't make the loss bigger.

**Fix**: Estimate what insurance would have paid and subtract. If the user wants to claim the entire loss, they must establish that no insurance would have covered it (e.g., specific exclusion, deductible higher than loss).

**Citation**: Pub 547 "Failure to file a claim"; Reg. §1.165-1(d)(2)(iii).

---

## 8. Mixing personal and business use without allocation

**The error**: Home office (10% business use) destroyed in a fire. User claims the entire loss in Section B (no floors).

**Why wrong**: Mixed-use property is allocated. The 10% business portion goes to Section B (no floor), the 90% personal portion goes to Section A (and only if a federally declared disaster).

**Fix**: Allocate by business-use percentage. Run two computations — one in Section B for the business share, one in Section A for the personal share.

**Citation**: Reg. §1.165-7; Pub 547 "Property Used Partly for Business and Partly for Personal Purposes".

---

## 9. Treating a theft loss as a Section A loss when no FEMA disaster applies

**The error**: User's laptop is stolen from their home (personal-use). They file Section A.

**Why wrong**: Theft is never a federally declared disaster. Post-TCJA, personal-use theft losses are not deductible at all (2018-2025).

**Fix**: If the property was used 100% in business, file Section B (no floors apply). If personal-use, the loss is not deductible. Confirm classification.

**Citation**: IRC §165(h)(5); Pub 547 "Theft Losses".

---

## 10. Missing the §1033 election when reimbursement creates a gain

**The error**: Insurance pays $250K for a $180K-basis home. User reports a $70K loss on Form 4684 (wrong direction) or recognizes the gain without §1033 deferral.

**Why wrong**: Reimbursement > basis is a GAIN, not a loss. The user can often defer under §1033 by reinvesting in similar replacement property within the replacement period.

**Fix**: Report the gain on Form 4684 Line 4 (Section A) or Line 22 (Section B). If the user is rebuilding/replacing, file the §1033 election statement to defer. Refer to a CPA — basis tracking is complex.

**Citation**: IRC §1033; Pub 547 "Gain From a Casualty or Theft".

---

## 11. Not retaining contemporaneous documentation

**The error**: User claims a $50K loss with no photos, no police report, no repair estimates, no insurance correspondence.

**Why wrong**: The IRS expects documentation. Pub 584 (personal) and Pub 584-B (business) are workbooks specifically designed for casualty/theft documentation. Lack of records doesn't bar the deduction but makes audit defense nearly impossible.

**Fix**: Retain all of:
- Photos of damaged property (before and after, if possible)
- Police report (theft, vandalism)
- Insurance claim correspondence (filing, settlement, denial)
- Repair estimates / contractor invoices
- Receipts establishing basis (original purchase, improvements)
- FEMA disaster registration (Section A)
- Appraisals supporting FMV before/after

Retain for at least 3 years after filing (longer for §1033 deferrals).

---

## 12. Filing Form 4684 when the user takes the standard deduction

**The error**: Section A casualty loss flows to Schedule A Line 15. User takes the standard deduction. Form 4684 is filed but produces no benefit.

**Why wrong**: Schedule A is only used if itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction. If standard is bigger, the casualty loss provides no tax benefit and the form is unnecessary.

**Fix**: Before filing Form 4684 Section A, compute itemized total (including the casualty loss) vs. standard deduction. If standard is bigger, skip Form 4684 — it's wasted work. Tell the user.

Note: Form 4684 Section B (business) flows to Form 4797 / Schedule C, not Schedule A — Section B is independent of standard-vs-itemized.

---

## Final review checklist (before declaring the form ready)

- [ ] FEMA declaration number entered if Section A used
- [ ] Each casualty event treated as one $100 reduction
- [ ] 10% AGI floor applied once across all Section A losses
- [ ] Adjusted basis used (not replacement cost or insured value)
- [ ] Insurance reimbursement subtracted (received OR reasonably expected)
- [ ] Mixed-use property allocated between Section A and Section B
- [ ] Theft of personal-use property NOT claimed (post-TCJA)
- [ ] Gain treatment used if reimbursement > basis (consider §1033)
- [ ] Documentation referenced and retained
- [ ] Itemized vs. standard deduction comparison run before filing Section A
- [ ] Disaster-year election (Section D) considered if federally declared
- [ ] Schedule A Line 15 / Line 16 cross-check
- [ ] Form 4797 Part II Line 14 cross-check (Section B business)
