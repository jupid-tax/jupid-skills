# Top Form 4562 Mistakes

Real mistakes filers make on Form 4562, ranked by how often they trigger IRS notices, audit adjustments, or recapture surprises. Each entry has the mistake, consequence, and fix.

---

## 1. Forgetting Form 4562 entirely when it's required

**The mistake**: User claims depreciation on Schedule C Line 13 without attaching Form 4562. Or claims §179 without filing Form 4562.

**Consequence**: IRS may disallow the entire Line 13 deduction as unsubstantiated. CP2000 notice is common.

**Fix**: Form 4562 is required if the taxpayer is:
- Claiming §179 in the current year
- Claiming bonus depreciation
- Claiming MACRS depreciation on assets placed in service in the current year
- Claiming any depreciation on listed property (vehicles, computers <100% business)
- Claiming amortization that began in the current year

It is NOT required if the only depreciation is on prior-year MACRS assets (with no current-year additions and no listed property). Small operational ambiguity exists for "continuing depreciation only" — when in doubt, attach.

---

## 2. Section 179 carryforward forgotten the next year

**The mistake**: Year 1, §179 limited by income, $20,000 carries forward (Line 13). Year 2, the user doesn't put $20,000 on Line 10 and loses the deduction.

**Consequence**: Permanent loss of $20,000 deduction unless the user catches it within the amendment window.

**Fix**: Track §179 carryforward in the user's records and add to Line 10 every year until fully absorbed. The carry has no expiration but only deducts up to each year's business income.

---

## 3. Cost basis on Line 6 not adjusted for business-use percentage

**The mistake**: $40,000 truck used 80% for business. User puts $40,000 on Line 6 column (b) and elects $40,000 §179 in column (c).

**Consequence**: Over-deducting; the personal 20% portion ($8,000) isn't deductible.

**Fix**: Line 6 (b) cost = full cost × business-use %. So $40,000 × 80% = $32,000. Maximum elected §179 in (c) = $32,000.

---

## 4. Claiming §179 + bonus on a passenger sedan, ignoring §280F caps

**The mistake**: $50,000 passenger sedan, 100% business use. User elects §179 $50,000 + bonus $0. Expects $50,000 first-year deduction.

**Consequence**: §280F luxury auto cap applies. 2025 first-year cap with bonus is ~$20,400. Excess $29,600 stuck in basis until post-recovery-period.

**Fix**: Pre-compute the §280F cap before electing §179. For passenger autos, the cap usually makes §179 + bonus pointless — the cap binds before either election helps. Consider: lease the sedan instead, or buy a >6,000 lb GVWR vehicle.

---

## 5. Heavy SUV §179 in excess of $31,300

**The mistake**: $80,000 heavy SUV (>6,000 lbs GVWR). User elects §179 $80,000 thinking they avoid §280F.

**Consequence**: §179 capped at $31,300 (2025) under IRC §179(b)(5). Excess must be bonus or MACRS.

**Fix**: §179 = $31,300, bonus depreciation = $48,700 (100% in 2025 post-Jan 19 under OBBBA). Total year 1 = $80,000 — fully expensed but properly split between §179 and bonus.

---

## 6. Listed property with ≤50% business use — claiming §179 or bonus

**The mistake**: User claims §179 on a computer used 40% for business.

**Consequence**: Listed property with ≤50% business use cannot use §179, bonus, or accelerated MACRS (IRC §280F(b)(1)). Must use ADS straight-line over the longer ADS recovery period. IRS will adjust on audit.

**Fix**: Verify business-use % before electing. If ≤50%, force ADS SL. If business use is borderline (e.g., 51%), document carefully — the IRS may scrutinize.

---

## 7. Section 179 election exceeding business income

**The mistake**: $40,000 net Schedule C profit before §179, user elects $50,000 §179.

**Consequence**: §179 limited to $40,000 (Line 11 income limit). $10,000 carries forward to Line 13. The user's tax software may flag, but DIY paper filers often miss it.

**Fix**: Pre-compute the income limit. If §179 exceeds it, either reduce the elected amount on Line 6, or accept the carryforward and remind the user. Note: bonus depreciation is NOT income-limited and can create a loss — if the user has low income, bonus is preferable to §179.

---

## 8. Bonus depreciation taken on real property over 20-year class

**The mistake**: User builds out a 39-year nonresidential office and claims bonus depreciation on it.

**Consequence**: 39-year nonresidential is NOT bonus-eligible (recovery period >20 years). Only **qualified improvement property** (interior improvements to nonresidential, treated as 15-year) gets bonus. IRS will adjust.

**Fix**: Distinguish "the building" (39-year, no bonus) from "qualified improvement property within the building" (15-year, bonus-eligible). A cost segregation study can identify QIP and other shorter-life components for bonus eligibility.

---

## 9. Amortizing §197 intangibles over actual useful life instead of 15 years

**The mistake**: User acquires a business with $50,000 customer list. User estimates customers turn over in 5 years and amortizes over 5 years.

**Consequence**: Over-deducting in years 1-5; under-deducting in years 6-15. IRS will adjust to 15-year SL on audit.

**Fix**: §197 is a statutory 15-year SL period regardless of actual useful life. No exceptions for "shorter actual life".

---

## 10. Forgetting the §179 recapture when business use drops

**The mistake**: Year 1: user §179s a $40,000 vehicle at 80% business use, deducts $32,000. Year 3: business use drops to 40%. User keeps depreciating but doesn't recapture.

**Consequence**: §179 recapture under IRC §280F(b)(2). Recapture amount = §179 + bonus + accelerated depreciation taken − ADS SL that would have been allowable. Reported on Form 4797 Part IV. Tax + 20% accuracy penalty + interest if discovered in audit.

**Fix**: Before electing §179 on listed property, project business use through the recovery period. If shaky, use straight MACRS or ADS — those don't trigger recapture from a use drop. Recapture is also triggered by gift, abandonment, or conversion to personal use.

---

## 11. Not separating Line 22 (depreciation) from Line 44 (amortization)

**The mistake**: User puts §197 amortization on Line 22 with depreciation. Line 22 flows to Schedule C Line 13.

**Consequence**: Amortization belongs on Line 44 and flows to Schedule C Line 27a (with Part V breakdown), not Line 13. Misclassification distorts NAICS-based ratios that the IRS uses to flag returns.

**Fix**: Form 4562 splits depreciation (Lines 14–22) from amortization (Lines 42–44) for a reason. Each flows to a different income tax return line. Track them separately on the asset register.

---

## 12. Mid-quarter convention triggered but not applied

**The mistake**: User buys $10,000 of equipment in Q1 and $30,000 in Q4 (75% in Q4 by cost). User uses half-year convention on all assets.

**Consequence**: Mid-quarter convention required because Q4 placements > 40% of year's personal-property purchases. All assets use MQ — different first-year percentages. IRS will adjust.

**Fix**: Compute the MQ test annually. If triggered, use MQ for ALL personal property (not just Q4 assets). The Q1 assets get a more favorable MQ percentage; the Q4 assets get a less favorable one. Pub. 946 has the MQ tables.

---

## 13. Vehicle Form 4562 Part V Line 30a ≠ Schedule C Line 9 mileage

**The mistake**: User claims 1,800 business miles on Schedule C Line 9 but writes 12,000 business miles on Form 4562 Part V Line 30a.

**Consequence**: Internal inconsistency; IRS notices. The same vehicle used for the same business should have the same business mileage on both forms.

**Fix**: For taxpayers using actual expenses (so Form 4562 is needed), the same business miles substantiate both depreciation and the actual-expense % used on Schedule C. Reconcile before filing.

For taxpayers using standard mileage rate, Form 4562 Part V is generally NOT filled out for that vehicle (the mileage rate already includes a depreciation component). The exception: if Form 4562 is required for other reasons (other assets, listed property), the vehicle still needs Part V Section B mileage but no Section A depreciation.

---

## 14. Bonus opt-out election omitted from return

**The mistake**: User wants to opt out of bonus depreciation for the 5-year class. User just doesn't put bonus on Line 14 and assumes that's enough.

**Consequence**: Default rule is bonus applies unless an affirmative written election out is attached to the return. Without the statement, IRS could deem bonus taken regardless. On audit, the difference could matter.

**Fix**: Attach a written statement to the timely-filed return: "Taxpayer [name, ID] elects out of the additional first-year depreciation under IRC §168(k)(7) for 5-year property placed in service during tax year [year]." The election is irrevocable.

---

## 15. Computer placed in service partway through the year, full year of depreciation taken

**The mistake**: User buys a $5,000 computer on Dec 15, 2025, takes a full year of MACRS depreciation as if placed in service Jan 1.

**Consequence**: Half-year convention applies for personal property; the asset is treated as placed in service at the year's midpoint, not Dec 15. Year 1 depreciation = 20% × $5,000 × ½ year = ½ × 20% × $5,000 = $500 effectively (half-year HY method already builds in the half year, so 20% × $5,000 = $1,000 total under HY for 5-year class). Confusion is common.

**Fix**: For 5-year property, half-year convention, year 1 percentage is 20% (the table value already includes the half-year). User shouldn't multiply by ½ again. If MQ applies (Q4 placement triggers MQ test), use the MQ table specific to the placement quarter — Q4 placements get only 5% in year 1.

---

## 16. Listed property "evidence" question answered untruthfully

**The mistake**: User has no mileage log but answers "Yes" to Line 24a (have evidence) and "Yes" to Line 24b (written).

**Consequence**: Audit risk. The IRS routinely asks for the log when auditing Form 4562 vehicle deductions; "Yes/Yes" with no log is at best taxpayer incompetence and at worst fraud.

**Fix**: Answer truthfully. If no log exists, "No" on 24a (evidence). The deduction is at risk in audit but answering falsely makes it worse. The skill should refuse to file the return with knowingly-false 24a/24b answers.

---

## 17. §195 start-up costs treated as $5K + 5-year amortization (instead of $5K + 180-month amortization)

**The mistake**: User has $30,000 of start-up costs. Deducts $5,000 in year 1, then amortizes $25,000 over 5 years.

**Consequence**: §195 amortization is 180 months (15 years), not 5 years. Over-deducting in years 1-5; under-deducting in years 6-15. IRS will adjust.

**Fix**: $5,000 first-year deduction (subject to phase-out at $50,000). Excess amortizes over 180 months SL.

---

## 18. Using Form 4562 to fix prior-year depreciation errors

**The mistake**: User realizes 2023 depreciation was wrong (used 39-year on a 27.5-year asset) and "fixes" it on the current-year Form 4562.

**Consequence**: Prior-year depreciation errors generally require **Form 3115** (Application for Change in Accounting Method) with a §481(a) adjustment, not a current-year Form 4562 fix. The "fix" on current-year 4562 is unauthorized.

**Fix**: For depreciation method/recovery period errors that span >1 year, use Form 3115 (DCN 7 for impermissible to permissible method change). The skill should redirect the user to the `form-3115` skill.

---

## 19. Confusing depreciation with cost of goods sold

**The mistake**: User treats inventory purchases as depreciable assets on Form 4562.

**Consequence**: Inventory is COGS (Schedule C Part III), not depreciation. IRS will reclassify and possibly question other categorizations.

**Fix**: Inventory items resold to customers are COGS. Long-lived assets used IN the business (machines, vehicles, computers) are depreciable. The skill must distinguish.

---

## 20. Misallocating cost in an asset acquisition (Form 8594)

**The mistake**: User buys a small business for $200,000. Allocates $50,000 to equipment (depreciable) and $150,000 to goodwill (15-year §197). Doesn't file Form 8594.

**Consequence**: Form 8594 is required (with both buyer and seller's tax returns) when an applicable asset acquisition occurs. Missing it triggers IRS scrutiny; the seller's allocation must match the buyer's. If allocations diverge, both parties may face adjustment.

**Fix**: File Form 8594 with both buyer and seller returns. The allocation determines depreciation/amortization for the buyer (Form 4562 entries) and gain/loss character for the seller (Form 4797). The skill should remind the user of Form 8594 when the user describes acquiring a business or substantial portion thereof.
