# Common-Law Worker Classification Test

The IRS uses a three-category common-law test to determine whether a worker is an employee or an independent contractor for federal employment-tax purposes. The framework is in **IRS Publication 15-A** (Employer's Supplemental Tax Guide), evolved from the older 20-factor test in **Rev. Rul. 87-41**.

For Form 8919 to apply, the worker must reasonably believe — based on the common-law test — that they are an employee, not a contractor. This document is the questionnaire the agent walks the user through before producing Form 8919.

**Legal basis:** IRC §3121(d)(2); IRS Pub 15-A; Rev. Rul. 87-41, 1987-1 C.B. 296.

---

## Category 1: Behavioral Control

Behavioral control covers facts showing whether the **business has the right to direct or control how the worker does the work**. Strong behavioral control = employee.

### Type of Instructions Given

A worker is more likely an **employee** if the firm tells them:

- When to do the work (set hours, shifts, schedules)
- Where to do the work (specific office, specific job site, specific times remote)
- What tools or equipment to use
- What workers to hire or to assist with the work
- Where to purchase supplies
- What work must be performed by a specified individual (i.e., the worker can't subcontract)
- What order or sequence to follow

A worker is more likely a **contractor** if the firm specifies only the end product or service, leaving methods to the worker.

**Questions for the user:**

- [ ] Did the firm set your work hours? (yes = employee)
- [ ] Did the firm require attendance at a specific location? (yes = employee)
- [ ] Did the firm dictate the tools, software, or equipment you must use? (yes = employee)
- [ ] Did you have to follow a specific procedure or methodology? (yes = employee)
- [ ] Could you delegate the work to a subcontractor of your choosing? (yes = contractor)
- [ ] Did the firm specify only the deliverable, leaving you to choose how to produce it? (yes = contractor)

### Degree of Instruction

More detailed instructions = more behavioral control = more employee-like. A firm that micromanages methods is treating the worker as an employee.

### Evaluation Systems

How does the firm evaluate the work?

- **Evaluating how the work is done** (process, methods, conduct) → employee
- **Evaluating only the end result** → contractor

### Training

Periodic or on-going training about procedures and methods → strong employee indicator. Independent contractors generally use their own methods and don't need (or want) the firm's training.

---

## Category 2: Financial Control

Financial control covers facts about **the business and financial aspects of the work**. Strong financial control by the firm = employee. Weak financial control by the firm (worker bears the financial risk) = contractor.

### Significant Investment

Did the worker make a significant investment in equipment, vehicles, software, or facilities used for the work?

- Significant investment by worker → contractor
- Firm provides everything → employee

### Unreimbursed Expenses

Does the worker incur unreimbursed expenses?

- Worker bears expenses (especially fixed ongoing expenses regardless of whether work is being performed) → contractor
- Firm reimburses expenses → employee

### Opportunity for Profit or Loss

Can the worker realize a profit or take a loss based on managerial skill?

- Can earn profit or suffer loss → contractor
- Paid a regular wage with no loss risk → employee

### Services Available to the Market

Does the worker offer services to the public or to other firms?

- Yes (other clients, advertises, has business cards/website) → contractor
- No (works exclusively for one firm) → employee

### Method of Payment

How is the worker paid?

- Regular wage (hourly, weekly, salary, retainer) regardless of project → employee
- By the project, with progress milestones → contractor
- Flat fee per deliverable → contractor

**Questions for the user:**

- [ ] Did you make a significant investment in tools or equipment for this work? (yes = contractor)
- [ ] Did the firm reimburse your expenses, or did you absorb them? (reimbursed = employee)
- [ ] Could you have lost money on the engagement? (yes = contractor)
- [ ] Did you have other clients during the engagement? (yes = contractor)
- [ ] Were you paid a regular wage regardless of output? (yes = employee)

---

## Category 3: Type of Relationship

Type of relationship covers facts about **how the parties perceive their relationship**. Strong indicators of an employment relationship = employee.

### Written Contracts

Is there a written contract describing the relationship?

- Contract says "independent contractor" → not dispositive (the IRS looks past contract labels to the actual relationship)
- Contract describes employment terms (benefits, regular wage, indefinite duration) → employee

The IRS rule is clear: **labels in a contract do not control if the actual relationship is one of employment.** You can't make a worker a contractor by writing "independent contractor" on a contract if the firm exercises behavioral and financial control.

### Employee Benefits

Does the firm provide any of the following?

- Health insurance, retirement plan, paid leave, life insurance → employee
- None of the above → contractor (consistent with contractor status)

The absence of benefits, however, does not by itself make a worker a contractor. Many misclassified workers receive no benefits; that's part of why misclassification is a problem.

### Permanency of the Relationship

How long is the engagement expected to last?

- Indefinite (no end date) → employee
- Specific project with defined end → contractor
- Long-term (multiple years) without defined end → strong employee indicator

### Services Provided as a Key Activity of the Business

Is the worker's role integral to the firm's business?

- Core activity of the firm → employee
- Peripheral or one-time activity → contractor

A web developer at a software company is doing the core activity → employee indicator. A web developer hired one-time to redesign a restaurant's website is doing a peripheral activity → contractor indicator.

**Questions for the user:**

- [ ] Was there a written contract? If so, what did it call the relationship? (label not dispositive)
- [ ] Did the firm offer any benefits? (yes = employee indicator)
- [ ] Was the engagement indefinite or for a specific project? (indefinite = employee)
- [ ] Was your work a key activity of the firm's business? (yes = employee)

---

## Scoring the Test

There is no fixed weighting of the three categories. The IRS looks at the **whole relationship** and the **degree to which each factor points to control or independence**. As a heuristic for the agent:

- If **two or three** categories clearly indicate employee → file Form 8919
- If **one** category indicates employee and the others are mixed → marginal; user may file 8919 with code G but should expect potential challenge
- If **zero or one** category indicates employee → file Schedule SE (genuine self-employment)

**When in doubt, file Form SS-8 and let the IRS rule.** Filing SS-8 with a good-faith case is not penalized; the IRS may rule contractor and the user files Schedule SE for that year.

---

## Documentation the Worker Should Keep

Whatever the test result, the worker should retain:

- Copies of all 1099 forms received
- The contract or engagement letter
- Email exchanges showing the firm's direction (hours, methods, supervision)
- Time records or timesheets if any
- Records of equipment provided by the firm
- Records of training received
- Notes on supervision and reviews
- Any HR-style communications (performance reviews, disciplinary actions)

This documentation supports both the SS-8 filing and any subsequent audit defense. **Retain for at least 3 years from filing the related 1040** (longer if audit risk is elevated).

## Special Cases

### Statutory Employees

Certain workers are **statutory employees** by law regardless of the common-law test:

- Drivers who deliver food, beverage, or laundry products
- Full-time life insurance sales agents
- Home workers paid for piecework
- Full-time traveling or city salespeople

These workers receive a W-2 with Box 13 "Statutory employee" checked. They do **not** need Form 8919 — they're already on a W-2.

### Statutory Non-Employees

Certain workers are **statutory non-employees** (treated as self-employed by law):

- Direct sellers
- Licensed real estate agents
- Certain companion sitters

These workers are **not** eligible for Form 8919 even if their work pattern looks like employment. Statutory rules override the common-law test.

### Section 530 Relief (Employer-Side)

Section 530 of the Revenue Act of 1978 provides relief to **employers** who consistently treated workers as contractors based on a reasonable basis. **It does not protect workers** — a worker can still file Form 8919 even if the employer has Section 530 protection. The Section 530 employer just won't owe back FICA; the worker pays the 7.65% via Form 8919, and the IRS doesn't pursue the employer for the matching share.

This is why Form 8919 exists at all: it's the worker's mechanism to pay only the employee share when the employer's matching share won't be collected.
