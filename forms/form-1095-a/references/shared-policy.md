# Shared Policy Allocation (Form 8962 Part IV)

When a single Form 1095-A covers individuals across two or more tax households, the policy amounts must be allocated. Form 8962 Part IV (Lines 30–34) handles this. Up to four allocations can appear.

## When does shared policy allocation apply?

Anyone listed on Form 1095-A Part II (Covered Individuals) who is NOT on the user's tax return triggers the allocation. Common scenarios:

### Scenario 1 — Divorced or separated parents

A child is covered under one parent's Marketplace policy, but the OTHER parent claims the child as dependent on the tax return. The covering parent has a 1095-A that includes the child, but the child isn't on the covering parent's tax return.

### Scenario 2 — Unmarried parents

Two unmarried parents each have their own tax return. Their child is covered on one parent's Marketplace policy. The parent who claims the child as dependent on their tax return does NOT have the child on their own 1095-A. Allocation is required.

### Scenario 3 — Adult child filing own return

A 23-year-old child is covered under the parent's Marketplace policy but files their own tax return (parent does NOT claim as dependent). The child's coverage on the parent's 1095-A must be allocated.

### Scenario 4 — Multiple unrelated taxpayers on one policy

Two unrelated adults share a household and were enrolled together. They file separate returns. Each gets a copy of the same 1095-A and must allocate.

---

## How to allocate

The taxpayers sharing the policy must agree on an allocation percentage. The allocation can differ for each of the three columns:

- Premium (Column A) allocation %
- SLCSP (Column B) allocation %
- APTC (Column C) allocation %

Each column's percentages **must total 100%** across all sharing taxpayers.

### Common allocations

**50/50 default**: If sharing taxpayers cannot agree, each takes 50% of all three columns. Most defensible in absence of an agreement.

**100/0 to one party**: If both sharing taxpayers agree, one takes 100% and the other takes 0%. The party taking 100% gets the full PTC reconciliation; the other party files no Form 8962 (or files showing 0% allocation).

**Asymmetric**: e.g., 60% to one and 40% to the other, or different percentages for different columns. Allowed but harder to defend in audit.

### Documenting the agreement

Best practice: get a written agreement signed by both parties before filing, and **both parties must use the same allocation on their respective Form 8962s**. Different allocations on different returns triggers IRS rejection.

## Filling Form 8962 Part IV

### Lines 30–33 — Up to four allocations

Each allocation row has six fields:
- (a) Policy number from Form 1095-A
- (b) SSN of other taxpayer
- (c) Allocation start month
- (d) Allocation stop month
- (e) Premium percentage allocation
- (f) SLCSP percentage allocation
- (g) APTC percentage allocation

If the same allocation applies all 12 months, use Jan / Dec for start / stop.

### Line 34 — Have you allocated all of Form 1095-A Part III?

Yes/No. "Yes" means you've accounted for 100% of the policy across yourself and the other taxpayer(s). If "No", you haven't allocated correctly.

### After allocation, return to Part II Lines 12–23

Each monthly column on Lines 12–23 (the (a), (b), (f) columns) is multiplied by your allocation percentage before entering. So if your premium allocation is 60%, and 1095-A January Column A = $1,200, you enter $720 (= $1,200 × 0.60) in Form 8962 Line 12 column (a).

Repeat for SLCSP (Column B → column (b)) and APTC (Column C → column (f)).

---

## Year-of-divorce special case

If you divorced during the year and were married at enrollment but file singly at year-end:
- Coverage months while married → both spouses' tax families share the policy → allocation needed
- Coverage months after divorce → each former spouse on separate return → may need partial-year allocation

This gets complex. See Pub 974 Chapter 4 "Allocation of Policy Amounts" for full rules.

---

## What happens if allocations don't match between returns?

The IRS receives both returns and compares Form 8962 Part IV. If allocations differ:
- Both returns are flagged for review
- Refunds may be delayed
- One or both filers may receive a CP2000 notice proposing adjustment

The way to avoid this: communicate with the other taxpayer before filing, agree on allocation, document the agreement, and both file using the same numbers.

## Allocation worksheet example

**Setup**: Two unmarried parents (David and Sarah) share a Marketplace policy that covers them and their daughter. Sarah claims daughter as dependent. David has 1095-A in his name; his Form 1095-A Part II shows David, Sarah, and daughter.

**They agree**: David takes 50% of premium and SLCSP; Sarah takes 50%. APTC paid was $400/month total — they split as 50% David, 50% Sarah. Daughter's allocation goes to Sarah (she's the dependent).

**David's Form 8962 Part IV Line 30**:
- (a) Policy number: 1234567890
- (b) Sarah's SSN: ###-##-####
- (c) Start month: 01
- (d) Stop month: 12
- (e) Premium %: 50%
- (f) SLCSP %: 50%
- (g) APTC %: 50%

**Sarah's Form 8962 Part IV Line 30**:
- (a) Policy number: 1234567890
- (b) David's SSN: ###-##-####
- (c) Start month: 01
- (d) Stop month: 12
- (e) Premium %: 50%
- (f) SLCSP %: 50%
- (g) APTC %: 50%

Both use the same numbers but identify each other as the "other taxpayer."
