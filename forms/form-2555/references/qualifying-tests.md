# Form 2555 Qualifying Tests — Bona Fide Residence vs. Physical Presence

To claim the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion, the filer must pass exactly ONE of two qualifying tests (IRC §911(d)(1)). The user picks which test fits their facts.

## Comparison summary

| | Bona Fide Residence (BFR) | Physical Presence (PPT) |
|---|--------------------------|-------------------------|
| Eligibility | US citizens (and resident aliens of treaty countries) | US citizens AND resident aliens |
| Test | Bona fide resident of foreign country for entire tax year | 330 full days in foreign country during any 12 consecutive months |
| Test type | Subjective (intent, integration) | Objective (count days) |
| Period | Must cover entire tax year (Jan 1 – Dec 31) | Any 12-month period |
| US visits | Allowed if foreign tax home maintained | Each US day reduces count toward 330 |
| First year flexibility | Cannot start mid-year (e.g., moving abroad in March = no FEIE for that year unless using PPT) | Can use a 12-month period that started mid-year; pro-rated |
| Foreign tax compliance | Filer should be a tax resident of foreign country | Not required to be tax resident |
| IRS audit difficulty | Higher (subjective factors) | Lower (count days) |

## Bona Fide Residence Test in detail

### What "bona fide residence" means

A bona fide resident is someone who:

- Has established a foreign country as their home for an indefinite period
- Has integrated into the local community (housing, family, social ties, civic participation)
- Files (or is liable to file) income tax in the foreign country as a tax resident
- Does not have abode in the US

The IRS evaluates BFR using factors from Reg. §1.911-2(c) and Sochurek v. Commissioner. Key factors:

1. **Intention** — does the filer intend to stay indefinitely (yes = BFR-friendly) or for a fixed short period (no = BFR-unfriendly)?
2. **Establishment of foreign home** — owned/leased home with family there (yes), rented short-term apartment alone (less so)
3. **Integration into foreign community** — local bank account, foreign tax filing, foreign driver's license, club memberships, language learning
4. **Type of foreign visa** — work visa or residence permit (yes), tourist visa or short-term work permit (no)
5. **Statement to foreign authorities** — if the filer told the foreign country they're not a tax resident (Line 13 of Form 2555), BFR likely fails
6. **Family location** — if family lives with the filer abroad (yes), if family is in US (concerning)
7. **Length of stay** — already 1+ year of stay supports BFR; first 6 months alone usually doesn't

### Critical mechanic — "entire tax year"

The BFR period must cover an entire tax year. Mechanic:

- Filer establishes BFR on August 15, 2024 (intent + factors at that moment)
- For tax year 2024: BFR did NOT cover the entire tax year (only Aug-Dec). User cannot use BFR for 2024.
  - Can the user use PPT for 2024? Maybe — count days. Probably yes if they moved abroad in August and stayed 5+ months without US visits.
- For tax year 2025: BFR covers all 365 days of 2025 (since BFR started in 2024 and is ongoing). User CAN use BFR for 2025.
- For tax year 2024 (retrospective claim): user can claim BFR for 2024 even though 2024 wasn't entirely covered when filed, IF in retrospect the BFR period spans an entire tax year. This is the "first year and last year" rule (IRC §911(d)(1)(A) parenthetical and Pub 54).

The "first year and last year" rule means: if BFR period is 2024-08-15 through 2026-04-30, the filer can claim BFR for tax years 2024, 2025, and 2026 — but the 2024 and 2026 portions are pro-rated based on days in foreign country during those tax years.

### When BFR fails

- Filer left the foreign country mid-year and returned to US permanently (BFR breaks)
- Filer claimed non-resident status in the foreign country to avoid foreign tax (Line 13 = Yes)
- Filer's stay was always intended to be short (e.g., 6-month assignment); BFR generally fails for clearly temporary stays
- Filer's family stayed in US, filer commuted home frequently — abode in US argument

### Audit defense

Keep:
- Foreign lease or property documents
- Foreign tax return showing tax residency
- Visa/residency permit
- Foreign driver's license, voter registration if applicable
- Bank statements showing foreign-based accounts
- Utility bills in filer's name at foreign address
- Letters from employer or local authorities
- Photos of foreign home (if audit gets serious)

## Physical Presence Test in detail

### Counting full days

A "full day" is a 24-hour period (midnight to midnight) entirely in a foreign country.

- Day arriving at a foreign country (e.g., land in Tokyo at 4pm): NOT a full day in Japan (the period from Jan 1 midnight to Jan 1 4pm was in transit, not in Japan)
- Day leaving (e.g., depart Japan at 10am): NOT a full day in Japan
- Day in transit between two foreign countries through international airspace (no US transit): full day abroad if the entire 24-hour period is over international waters/airspace; may be debatable — IRS often counts as foreign day
- Day in international waters not en route: full day abroad
- Day in international airspace transiting through US (e.g., LHR-JFK-LAX): NOT a full day abroad (US transit)
- Day partially in US, partially abroad (e.g., morning in Mexico, evening crossing into Texas): NOT a full day abroad

### The 12-month qualifying period

Any 12 consecutive months. The user picks the window. Common patterns:

- Calendar year: Jan 1 - Dec 31 (most common for full-year qualifiers)
- Mid-year-to-mid-year: e.g., March 15, 2024 - March 14, 2025 (when filer moved abroad in March)
- Multiple windows: a filer who's been abroad multiple years can pick any 12-month window with ≥ 330 days

The window is selected to MAXIMIZE the qualifying days within the tax year. Tax software does this automatically; manual filers must consciously pick.

### Pro-ration for partial-year qualifiers

If the qualifying 12-month window covers only part of the tax year, the FEIE cap pro-rates:

```
Pro-rated cap = Annual FEIE cap × (qualifying days within tax year / 365)
```

Example: filer moved to Brazil on March 15, 2024. Qualifying period is March 15, 2024 - March 14, 2025. Within tax year 2024 (Mar 15 - Dec 31), there are 292 days.

- Pro-rated 2024 cap = $126,500 × (292 / 365) = $101,194
- Pro-rated 2025 cap = $130,000 × ((365 - (March 14 → March 15) days) / 365) — count days of qualifying period in 2025

For 2025, the qualifying period extends to March 14, 2025. So days in tax year 2025 covered by the qualifying period: Jan 1 - March 14, 2025 = 73 days.
- Pro-rated 2025 cap based on this window = $130,000 × (73 / 365) = $26,000

But the filer can ALSO start a NEW qualifying window for 2025 (e.g., Jan 1 - Dec 31, 2025) if they continued abroad. Then 2025 = full $130,000.

The agent should help the user pick the optimal window for each tax year.

### Days actually counted toward 330

| Activity | Counts as full day abroad? |
|----------|---------------------------|
| 24 hours entirely in Mexico City | Yes |
| Land in Mexico at 11pm; 1 hour of Dec 31 abroad | No |
| Leave Mexico at 11pm; 1 hour of Dec 31 in transit | No |
| Cruise from Mexico to Brazil over international waters, 24 hours | Yes (if no US ports) |
| Cruise stops at Miami port for shore leave | The Miami-port day is NOT abroad |
| Layover in JFK during Tokyo-Miami flight | The day with US transit is NOT abroad |
| 24 hours in Brazil's territorial waters | Yes |
| 24 hours on a US-flagged vessel in international waters | Disputed; IRS may treat as US |
| Sleeping in foreign hotel night before / after travel | Helps if 24-hour period entirely abroad |

### Documentation for PPT

Keep:
- Passport with entry/exit stamps
- Boarding passes / e-tickets for every flight
- Foreign hotel receipts
- Foreign rental agreements
- Foreign employer payslips
- Credit card statements showing foreign location

The IRS audits PPT by reconstructing days from passport stamps and flight records. Missing stamps (e.g., Schengen Area where stamps aren't always given) may require additional substantiation.

## Choosing between tests

Use BFR when:
- The filer has lived abroad a year or more
- They have a clear foreign tax residency
- They want flexibility on US visits (vacations, family events)
- They have family in the foreign country

Use PPT when:
- The filer just moved abroad and BFR factors aren't yet fully established
- The filer's stay is finite/temporary (1-2 year assignment)
- The filer travels heavily but spends most time outside the US
- The filer wants the cleanest, most defensible test for IRS scrutiny

For a digital nomad (no fixed foreign tax home), PPT is generally the only viable test.

## Tie-breakers and edge cases

### Mid-year move

Filer moves to Spain in May 2024. For 2024:
- BFR fails (didn't cover entire 2024 tax year)
- PPT may work if they had ≥ 330 foreign days in any 12-month period extending into 2025

For 2025: BFR works if BFR continuous. PPT also works if they continued abroad.

### Returns to US for medical/family emergency

A short trip to US for family emergency or medical doesn't break BFR if the filer returns abroad and intends to remain. PPT counts those US days against the 330.

### Federal worker disqualification

US federal civilian workers stationed abroad often have abode in the US (federal stationing implies eventual return) and fail FEIE. Military pay has separate rules (combat zone exclusions, etc.).

### Working remotely for US employer while traveling

Critical fact: WHERE were the services performed? If the filer worked from a Bali co-working space, services were performed in Indonesia (foreign country) — qualifies as FEI. If the filer worked from a hotel in Mexico while their tax home stayed in Texas, abode-in-US may disqualify.

Digital nomads with no fixed home anywhere face the trickiest analysis. They typically use PPT (count days abroad) and argue tax home is wherever they spent the most time. The IRS has been increasingly skeptical of "rolling tax home" claims.

### Moving back to US

The year of return: BFR ends, PPT count breaks. The exclusion pro-rates to days the filer was abroad/qualified during the partial year.
