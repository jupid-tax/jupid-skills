# Form 2290 — Worked Example: Sarah, Ranch Owner with Cattle Hauler (Agricultural Suspension)

End-to-end Form 2290 filing for a single-vehicle agricultural operation under the suspension category at the 7,500-mile threshold.

---

## Persona

**Sarah** owns a 600-acre cattle ranch in Montana. She has a single hauling truck — a 2018 Kenworth T800 — that she uses about 4 times a year to take cattle to auction in Billings (~120 miles each way).

- Truck: 2018 Kenworth T800
- Taxable gross weight: 56,000 lbs (Category B)
- Use: **Agricultural** — exclusively transporting cattle (her own livestock) to auction
- Estimated annual mileage on public highways: ~4,000 miles (well under the 7,500 agricultural threshold)
- Truck is registered with Montana DMV as a "highway motor vehicle used for farming purposes"
- EIN: 45-1234567 (registered to her ranch operation, Big Sky Ranch LLC, taxed as sole proprietorship via SMLLC)
- Files Schedule F (farming income) on her personal Form 1040

## Why Agricultural Suspension Applies

Sarah's situation meets all three criteria for agricultural use under IRC §4483(d):

1. **Used primarily for farming** — exclusively for transporting her own cattle (an agricultural commodity)
2. **Registered as agricultural** under Montana state law
3. **Expected miles ≤ 7,500** during the period — Sarah projects 4,000 miles, well under the threshold

She files Form 2290 with the truck in **Category W** and pays $0 tax.

## Filing Workflow

1. **August 10, 2025** — Sarah receives an annual reminder to file Form 2290
2. **August 12, 2025** — Sarah logs into a 2290 e-file provider (chose ExpressTruckTax)
3. **Provider workflow:**
   - Confirms business name (Big Sky Ranch LLC), EIN, Montana address
   - Selects tax period 2025-2026
   - Adds vehicle: VIN, Category W (suspended), agricultural flag = Yes, first-use July 2025
   - Provider auto-generates Part II Statement (a) certification
   - Sarah reviews and digitally signs
   - Tax due: $0
   - No payment method needed (since tax is $0)
   - Submits filing
4. **Within 5 minutes** — IRS accepts; stamped Schedule 1 PDF returned (showing $0 tax, Category W)
5. **August 12, 2025** — Sarah saves stamped Schedule 1:
   - Cloud storage backup
   - Print copy for ranch office
6. **September 2025** — Sarah renews Montana plates with the agricultural designation; uploads stamped Schedule 1 to MT DMV portal; renewal approved

## Mileage Tracking Plan

Because Sarah is filing under suspension, she must track miles to ensure she stays under 7,500. Her plan:

- **Notebook in glove compartment** — date, route, miles for each trip
- **Monthly summary** transferred to a spreadsheet
- **Threshold alert at 6,500 miles** — gives 1,000 miles of buffer to file an Amended Return if needed

Sarah's typical year:

## What If Mileage Exceeds 7,500?

If Sarah unexpectedly crosses 7,500 miles (say, a year with extra hauling for neighbors or expanded operations):

1. **File Amended Return** (Form 2290, Box B checked) within the month following when the threshold was crossed
2. **Pay full annual tax** for Category B (56,000 lbs): $122
3. **Receive updated stamped Schedule 1** showing Category B (no longer suspended)
4. **Forward updated Schedule 1** to Montana DMV

If she crossed the threshold in May 2026, she'd file the Amended Return by June 30, 2026, and pay $122. If she crossed in February 2026, she'd file by March 31, 2026.

Note: In some cases, the partial-period rule applies to the original first-use month, not to the amendment date. Verify with the current Form 2290 instructions before computing the amended tax.

## At Tax Time (Schedule F for Tax Year 2025)

Sarah files Schedule F (Profit or Loss From Farming), not Schedule C, because she's a farmer. Her HVUT is $0, so there's nothing to deduct this year.

If she had paid tax (e.g., next year if she crosses the threshold), the HVUT would go on **Schedule F, Line 29 (Taxes)**.

The truck's other expenses (fuel, repairs, insurance, depreciation) go elsewhere on Schedule F per the standard farm expense lines.

## Lessons This Example Illustrates

1. **Suspension is filed, not skipped** — Sarah files Form 2290 even though she owes $0
2. **7,500-mile threshold for agricultural use** is higher than the 5,000-mile general rule
3. **State agricultural registration matters** — she must be registered under Montana law as a farming-purposes vehicle
4. **Stamped Schedule 1 still required** — the DMV needs proof of HVUT compliance even at $0
5. **Mileage tracking is non-optional** — without it, she can't defend the suspension if questioned, and can't catch an overage in time to amend
6. **Schedule F, not Schedule C** — farmers file Schedule F; ranchers, dairy operators, and crop growers use this form
