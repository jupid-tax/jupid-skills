# Sport Fishing and Archery Manufacturer's Excise Tax (§4161)

IRC §4161 imposes a manufacturer's excise tax on sport fishing equipment and archery equipment. The tax is paid by the manufacturer, producer, or importer at the **first sale** to an unrelated party. Retail sellers (sporting goods stores) generally don't pay; they pass through the manufacturer's price already including tax.

---

## Sport fishing equipment (§4161(a))

### Tax base and rate

| Item | IRS No. | Rate | Source |
|------|---------|------|--------|
| Most sport fishing equipment (rods, reels, lures, lines, nets, etc.) | 41 | 10% of sales price | §4161(a)(1) |
| Fishing tackle boxes | 42 | 3% of sales price | §4161(a)(1)(A) |
| Electric outboard motors | 110 | 3% of sales price | §4161(a)(1)(B) |
| Sonar devices that can identify fish (≤ $200 retail) | (taxable as "sport fishing equipment") | 10% | §4161(a)(2)(B) |

### What's taxable

- Fishing rods (any material, any technique)
- Fishing reels (spinning, casting, fly, etc.)
- Artificial lures, plugs, jigs, flies
- Fishing line (mono, fluoro, braid, fly line, leader material)
- Fishing tackle boxes (3% rate, not 10%)
- Fishing nets, gaffs, fish stringers
- Electric outboard motors (3% rate)
- Bait casting, fly fishing, ice fishing equipment

### What's NOT taxable

- Live or natural bait (worms, minnows, etc.)
- Boats themselves (even fishing boats)
- Gas-powered outboard motors (only ELECTRIC are §4161(a)(1)(B))
- Boat-mounted fish finders > $200 retail (per §4161(a)(2)(B) exception for high-end electronics)
- Generic outdoor equipment not fishing-specific
- Resold fishing equipment (manufacturer's tax already paid by original seller)
- Used / second-hand equipment

### Sales price computation

Sales price = price at first sale to unrelated party, **excluding**:
- The §4161 tax itself (a meta-rule: the tax isn't on top of itself)
- Federal retailers' excise (none on sport fishing — but note for other categories)
- Charges for warranty, transportation if separately stated and reasonable

For **related-party transfers** (parent-subsidiary), the IRS uses a "constructive sale price" — typically the fair market arm's-length price. Manufacturers transferring to controlled subsidiaries should use the established benchmark price.

### Schedule A reporting

Sport fishing manufacturers' tax IS subject to the semi-monthly liability reporting on Schedule A of Form 720. The liability is recognized at the time of sale (per §4161(a)).

For each sale during the quarter, the date determines which semi-monthly period the liability falls in:

| Period | Date range |
|--------|-----------|
| 1 | Day 1 – 15 of month 1 of quarter |
| 2 | Day 16 – end of month 1 |
| ... | ... |

The agent populates Schedule A with totals per period.

### Common sport fishing scenarios

1. **Domestic manufacturer sells to retailer**: 10% (or 3% for tackle boxes) of sales price at first sale. File Form 720 quarterly with Schedule A.

2. **Importer brings finished rods from China**: importer is treated as manufacturer. Tax is at first sale to unrelated US buyer (10%). File Form 720.

3. **E-commerce store buys components, assembles rods, sells direct-to-consumer**: the assembler is the manufacturer. Tax is on the consumer-facing price (10%). File Form 720.

4. **Retail store buys rods from manufacturer**: retail store does NOT file; manufacturer already paid §4161 tax embedded in price. (Retail store still pays state sales tax on resale, but that's not Form 720.)

5. **Used / second-hand rods**: not taxable (only first sale by manufacturer is subject to §4161).

## Archery equipment (§4161(b))

### Tax base and rate

| Item | IRS No. | Rate | Source |
|------|---------|------|--------|
| Bows of draw weight ≥ 30 lbs | 44 | 11% of sales price | §4161(b)(1) |
| Quivers, broadheads, points (ferrules, tips that attach to arrows) | 119 | 11% of sales price | §4161(b)(2)(B) |
| Arrows (per arrow shaft) | 106 | $0.59 per shaft (verify rate; tied to inflation under §4161(b)(2)(A) since 2007) | §4161(b)(2)(A) |
| Compound bows, recurve bows, longbows ≥ 30 lb draw weight | 44 | 11% | §4161(b)(1) |

### What's taxable

- Bows ≥ 30 lb draw weight (compound, recurve, longbow, traditional, takedown)
- Arrows (charged per shaft, NOT per assembled arrow)
- Quivers, broadheads, arrow points (ferrules)
- Bow-mounted accessories that are integral to the bow's function (some sights, stabilizers — verify by IRS Reg. §48.4161(b))
- Crossbows? — generally NOT subject to §4161 (different tax framework historically; verify current status)

### What's NOT taxable

- Bows < 30 lb draw weight (children's bows, beginner bows)
- Bowstrings sold separately (typically not taxable; verify)
- Targets, target stands, archery clothing
- Arrow rests, sights, stabilizers if sold separately and not "integral"
- Complete arrow assemblies — wait, this is taxable, but on a per-shaft basis. The shaft itself is taxed; assembled arrow is treated as shaft + components, with each component potentially separately taxable.

### Per-arrow shaft rate (§4161(b)(2)(A))

The arrow shaft tax is set at $0.59 per shaft as of recent years (verify current rate annually — it's tied to inflation under the statute). The tax applies to:

- Wooden, aluminum, fiberglass, carbon, or composite shafts
- Sold by themselves OR as part of an assembled arrow
- ≥ 18" length (excludes short crossbow bolts in some interpretations)

The rate is per shaft, not percentage of price. A $5 shaft and a $50 shaft both incur $0.59 tax.

### Schedule A reporting

Archery tax is also subject to semi-monthly liability reporting on Schedule A. Same mechanics as sport fishing.

### Common archery scenarios

1. **Bow manufacturer sells compound bow ≥ 30 lb**: 11% of sales price. File Form 720.

2. **Bow manufacturer sells youth bow at 25 lb draw**: NOT taxable (under 30 lb threshold). No §4161 tax.

3. **Arrow shaft producer sells 1,000 carbon shafts at $4 each = $4,000 sale**: tax = 1,000 × $0.59 = $590. NOT 11% of $4,000.

4. **Importer of archery components sells assembled arrows at $100/dozen**: tax = (12 shafts × $0.59) + 11% × value of taxable components (broadheads, quivers if attached). Per-component tax adds up.

5. **Custom bow builder sells 1 bow at $1,500**: 11% × $1,500 = $165 tax. File Form 720.

## Joint scenarios (sport fishing + archery)

A retailer that imports both fishing rods and bows files ONE Form 720 with both IRS Numbers (41 and 44 respectively). Each line is computed separately; both lines feed Part III.

## Common §4161 mistakes

1. **Forgetting Schedule A**: §4161 categories require semi-monthly liability tracking. Filing without Schedule A can trigger §6656 deposit penalties even when total tax is paid.

2. **Wrong rate for tackle boxes**: tackle boxes are 3%, not 10%. Easy to miss in the catalog.

3. **Wrong rate for electric outboard motors**: electric outboard = 3% (§4161(a)(1)(B)), NOT 10%. Gas outboards aren't taxed at all.

4. **Counting bows < 30 lb as taxable**: the 30-lb draw weight threshold excludes most beginner / youth bows.

5. **Charging 11% on the assembled arrow price instead of $0.59 per shaft**: arrows are per-shaft, not percent.

6. **Constructive sale price for related-party transfers**: a manufacturer that sells to its own subsidiary at a deeply discounted "transfer price" can be challenged by the IRS for under-stating the tax base. Use arm's-length price.

7. **Used/second-hand confusion**: §4161 applies only to first sale by manufacturer/importer. Resale is not subject.

8. **State sales tax confusion**: the federal manufacturer's tax (§4161) is separate from state sales tax. Both may apply.

## Audit defense for §4161 filers

The IRS audits manufacturer's excise tax periodically. Common documentation needed:

1. Sales records showing first-sale price and date
2. Customer list with related-party flags
3. Schedule A liability tracking by semi-monthly period
4. Form 720 quarterly filings with EFTPS deposit confirmations
5. Inventory records distinguishing taxable vs non-taxable items
6. For arrows: shaft count records (audited to per-shaft tax)
7. For bows: draw weight specifications confirming ≥ 30 lb threshold
8. For tackle boxes: classification justification (3% vs 10%)

The agent should remind the user that the §4161 tax has a 3-year statute of limitations under §6501(a) — file accurately and keep records for 3+ years from filing.
