# Medical Expenses (Lines 1-4) — IRC §213 and Pub 502

Medical and dental expenses are deductible only to the extent they exceed **7.5% of AGI**. The 7.5% floor has been permanent since the Further Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2020. Earlier confusion about a 10% floor (under TCJA before 2019) is no longer relevant.

For most filers under 65 with employer health insurance and modest out-of-pocket spending, Lines 1-4 produce a $0 deduction — the 7.5% floor wipes everything out. The deduction primarily benefits filers with major medical events (long hospital stays, ongoing treatments, long-term care) or retirees with high premiums on Medicare supplements.

This file is the operational reference for what counts as a qualified medical expense, how the floor works, and what mistakes to avoid.

---

## The 7.5% AGI floor

```
Line 1: Total qualified medical expenses paid (out of pocket)
Line 2: AGI from Form 1040 Line 11
Line 3: 7.5% × Line 2
Line 4: max(0, Line 1 − Line 3)
```

At $100,000 AGI, the first $7,500 of medical expenses doesn't deduct. At $200,000 AGI, $15,000.

## What qualifies (per Pub 502)

Medical expenses are amounts paid for the **diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease**, or for the purpose of affecting any structure or function of the body.

### Healthcare professionals

- Physicians, surgeons, dentists, orthodontists
- Chiropractors, podiatrists, optometrists, audiologists
- Psychiatrists, psychologists, licensed therapists
- Nurses, including private-duty nursing care for the patient
- Acupuncturists (deductible under IRS Rev. Rul. 72-593)
- Physical and occupational therapists

### Treatment

- Inpatient hospital care
- Surgery (non-cosmetic)
- Mental health treatment (inpatient or outpatient)
- Dental work (fillings, extractions, dentures, orthodontia)
- Vision (eye exams, glasses, contacts, LASIK)
- Hearing aids and batteries
- Fertility treatments, including IVF
- Smoking-cessation programs (not OTC nicotine patches without prescription)
- Weight-loss programs only if for a *specific disease* diagnosed by a physician (obesity counts; general health does not)

### Prescription drugs

- Prescription medications and insulin
- Over-the-counter drugs are NOT deductible unless prescribed by a doctor (for HSA/FSA, OTC was made eligible without prescription by the CARES Act 2020 — but that's HSA/FSA reimbursement, not Schedule A deduction)

### Insurance premiums

- Medicare Part B, C, D premiums
- Long-term care insurance premiums (subject to age-based caps below)
- Private health insurance premiums **NOT** paid through pre-tax payroll deduction
- Health-share / Christian health-sharing ministry contributions are NOT deductible (IRS hasn't recognized them as insurance)

**Self-employed**: Health insurance premiums for the owner go on **Schedule 1 Line 17 (above-the-line)**, NOT Schedule A. Don't double-deduct.

**Pre-tax payroll premiums**: Already excluded from W-2 box 1; don't deduct again.

### Long-term care insurance — age caps for 2025

| Age at year-end | 2025 cap |
|------------------|----------|
| 40 or under | $480 |
| 41-50 | $900 |
| 51-60 | $1,800 |
| 61-70 | $4,810 |
| 71+ | $6,020 |

(Source: Rev. Proc. 2024-40 §3.27. Verify 2026 figures against the IRS Rev. Proc. for that year.)

### Capital expenses for medical purposes

Home modifications for a medical condition (wheelchair ramps, widened doorways, stair lifts, modified bathrooms) are deductible only to the extent the cost **exceeds** the amount it increases the home's value. If a $20,000 ramp adds $4,000 of resale value, $16,000 deducts.

Common medical capital expenses with often-zero increase to home value (fully deductible):
- Wheelchair ramps
- Stair lifts and elevators (if removed at sale, fully deductible)
- Grab bars in bathrooms
- Modified door handles, lower cabinets
- Air filtration systems for documented respiratory conditions

Get a written appraisal documenting the value impact for any modification > $5,000.

### Travel for medical care

- Mileage at the IRS medical mileage rate (21¢/mile for 2025 per Notice 2025-5; 2026 rate verified at https://www.irs.gov/tax-professionals/standard-mileage-rates)
- Parking and tolls
- Bus, train, taxi, ride-share to medical appointments
- Air travel for medical care if necessary
- Lodging at $50/night/person (capped) when travel is primarily for and essential to medical care provided at a licensed medical facility — meals during this lodging do NOT deduct (different from business travel)

A parent traveling with a sick child counts both as medical travel.

### Eligible dependents

The user can deduct medical expenses for:
- Self
- Spouse (filing MFJ; for MFS only the spouse who actually paid)
- Dependents claimed on the return
- A person who would have been a dependent but for income or filing-status tests (special rule for elderly parents whose income exceeds the dependent threshold)

Children of divorced parents: the parent who *pays* the medical expense deducts, regardless of which parent claims the dependency. Document with canceled checks or credit card statements.

## What does NOT qualify

- **Cosmetic surgery** unless correcting a deformity, congenital abnormality, or trauma injury or disfiguring disease (IRC §213(d)(9)). Botox, hair transplants, elective dental veneers — not deductible.
- **Gym memberships** (unless prescribed by physician for a specific disease, and even then IRS scrutiny is high)
- **Nutritional supplements, vitamins, herbal supplements** without prescription
- **OTC drugs** without prescription (note: HSA/FSA reimbursement allowed under CARES, but Schedule A deduction is not)
- **Funeral, burial, cremation** expenses
- **Toiletries, cosmetics, hygiene products**
- **Maternity clothes**
- **Dancing lessons, swimming lessons** (general fitness)
- **Medical marijuana** — NOT deductible federally even where state-legal (federally Schedule I substance, IRS following federal classification)
- **Veterinary fees** except for service animals trained to assist a disabled person
- **Health-sharing ministry contributions** (Christian health share, etc.) — IRS has not recognized as insurance
- **Premiums paid pre-tax** through employer payroll deduction (already excluded from W-2 box 1)

## Reimbursements: subtract before entering Line 1

Line 1 is the **out-of-pocket** amount. Subtract from gross expenses:

- Insurance reimbursements (commercial, Medicare, Medicaid)
- HSA distributions for medical expenses (you've already gotten tax-free treatment)
- FSA distributions (already pre-tax)
- HRA reimbursements (already pre-tax)
- Employer reimbursement (any kind)

Common mistake: deducting the full $10,000 surgery bill when insurance paid $8,000. Only $2,000 goes on Line 1.

## Special situations

### Medicare tax withholding

The Medicare portion of FICA withheld from W-2 box 6 is NOT a medical expense. It's a federal tax not deductible anywhere on Schedule A.

### Medicare premiums paid by Social Security

Medicare Part B premiums deducted from a Social Security check are deductible on Line 1. The amount appears on the year-end SSA-1099 statement (box 3 portion). Subtract from gross Social Security income to get net, but the premium itself is medical for Schedule A.

### Long-term care expenses for a chronically ill person

Qualified long-term care services for a person certified as chronically ill (IRC §7702B) deduct in full as medical, not subject to the LTC insurance premium age caps (those caps apply only to *insurance premiums*, not *services*).

### Marriage / dependent timing

Medical expenses are deductible by the person who **paid**. If filing MFJ, joint payment = joint deduction. If filing MFS, the paying spouse deducts.

### Year-end timing

Medical is cash-basis only. Expense paid by check on December 31 deducts in that year (when the check clears, IRS treats it as paid when mailed). Credit card charge on December 31 deducts that year (not when the credit card bill is paid).

### Insurance-paid-then-disputed

If insurance pays a claim, then later disputes and reverses the payment in a future year, the original year's deduction stays — and the recovery in the future year is income (tax-benefit rule).

## Substantiation

Required records:
- Receipts for every expense claimed
- Insurance EOBs showing the unreimbursed portion
- For prescription medications, the pharmacy printout or year-end pharmacy summary
- For mileage, a contemporaneous log: date, destination, miles, purpose
- For capital home improvements, a contractor's invoice plus an appraisal showing the value impact (if claiming the difference)
- For dependents whose medical the user paid, proof of payment from user's account

Keep records for 3 years from filing date (default IRS audit window). For long-term care insurance and capital home improvements, longer retention is advisable.

## Common medical mistakes

1. **Deducting cosmetic procedures** — IRC §213(d)(9) bars cosmetic. Get a doctor's letter establishing medical necessity if the procedure is borderline (e.g., breast reconstruction post-mastectomy is medical; elective augmentation is not).
2. **Forgetting the 7.5% floor** — at $100K AGI, the first $7,500 wipes out. Most filers without major medical events deduct $0.
3. **Double-counting HSA/FSA** — those are pre-tax. Don't deduct again.
4. **Including pre-tax payroll premiums** — already excluded from box 1. Look at the W-2 box 12 codes (DD = employer health coverage cost; not a deduction either way).
5. **Counting full medical bill instead of out-of-pocket** — only the unreimbursed portion deducts.
6. **Self-employed deducting health insurance on Schedule A** — that goes on Schedule 1 Line 17 instead. Don't do both.
7. **Including OTC drugs without prescription** — not deductible without prescription (Schedule A is stricter than HSA/FSA on this).
8. **Including health-sharing ministry contributions** — not recognized by IRS as insurance.
9. **Using business mileage rate (70¢) instead of medical rate (21¢)** — they're different for a reason.
10. **Treating commute to a job at a hospital as medical mileage** — it's not. Medical mileage is only for trips primarily for medical care.
